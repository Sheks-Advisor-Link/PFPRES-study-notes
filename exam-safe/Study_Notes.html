0. How an Estate Works — The Big Picture Foundation ▼

What is a "Deceased Estate"?

When someone dies in South Africa, all their assets and liabilities come together to form a legal entity called a deceased estate. This estate is a separate legal person in the eyes of the law — it can sue, be sued, own property, and owe debts. The estate exists from the moment of death until it is fully wound up (usually 6 months to 3 years later). The entire process is regulated by the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965 and supervised by the Master of the High Court.

The 7-Step Process of Winding Up an Estate

  1. Death & reporting: The death is reported to the Master of the High Court within 14 days. Required documents include the Death Notice (Form J294), death certificate, original will (if any), inventory (Form J243), Next of Kin Affidavit (Form J192), and Acceptance of Trust forms (Form J155).
  2. Executor appointed: The Master issues Letters of Executorship (for estates over R250,000) or Letters of Authority (for estates under R250,000 — the simpler "Section 18(3)" process). The executor is typically named in the will; otherwise nominated by heirs.
  3. Gather & value: Executor identifies, collects, and values all assets at market value at date of death. Advertises for creditors in the Government Gazette and a local newspaper (30 days to lodge claims).
  4. Pay debts, taxes & costs: Funeral costs, debts, CGT (on death), income tax (to date of death), executor's fees, Master's fees, and estate duty are all settled from estate funds.
  5. Liquidation & Distribution Account (L&D): The executor drafts the L&D account detailing all assets, liabilities, costs, and proposed distributions. This is lodged with the Master, who has about 2 months to examine it and raise any queries.
  6. Public advertisement: The L&D account is advertised to lie open for public inspection for 21 days at the Master's office and the relevant Magistrate's court.
  7. Distribution & discharge: Once approved and objections resolved, the executor distributes remaining assets to heirs per the will or intestate succession. The estate is then closed.

Why Estate Planning Matters

Without proper planning, an estate can face serious problems:

  • No will (dying intestate): Your assets are distributed per the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987 — which may not match your wishes (e.g. a life partner you aren't married to may get nothing).
  • Liquidity shortfall: Estate owes taxes & costs but has no cash — family assets (like the home or business) may have to be sold at fire-sale prices.
  • Estate duty exposure: Without structuring (trusts, insurance, spouse bequests), up to 25% of large estates can be lost to estate duty.
  • Contested claims: A surviving spouse left out of a will may launch a maintenance claim, delaying distribution to other heirs.
  • Business disruption: Without a buy-and-sell agreement, a business can fail when a shareholder dies.

The Four Pillars of This Module

Everything in this study guide fits into four broad areas:

  1. Technical foundations: Marital regimes, wills, intestate succession, trusts — the legal framework within which estates operate.
  2. Tax on death: Estate duty, CGT on death, income tax to date of death — the tax consequences of dying.
  3. Risk management tools: Life insurance, buy-and-sell agreements, retirement funds — the products used to address risks.
  4. Analysis & planning: Liquidity analysis, capital needs analysis — the calculations that tie everything together and identify shortfalls.
Summary: When you die, the law treats all your stuff (and your debts) as one bundle called your "estate". Someone (the executor) is put in charge of sorting it out — paying what you owe, paying taxes, and then giving what's left to the people named in your will (or, if you didn't have one, to whoever the law says). Estate planning is about making sure this process is smooth, tax-efficient, and matches what you actually want to happen.

Each topic in this guide explained in one short paragraph, no jargon. If you only had 10 minutes to understand the whole module, read this.

Marital Regimes

When you get married in South Africa you're picking one of three "systems" for how your stuff will be shared. In community of property means everything you each own gets combined into one pot — great for sharing, bad if one of you is financially reckless. Out of community, no accrual means you each keep your own stuff — great for keeping things separate, bad if one spouse gave up a career for the family. Out of community with accrual (the most common choice today) keeps you separate during the marriage but shares the growth when the marriage ends. Default is COP if you didn't sign an ANC (antenuptial contract) before the wedding.

Estate Duty

When you die, South Africa charges a tax on your "estate" (everything you owned). You get the first R3.5 million free (the "abatement"). Anything above that is taxed at 20% (up to R30M) or 25% (above R30M). But here's the trick: anything you leave to your spouse is completely exempt (s4(q) deduction) — you can leave a R100M estate to your wife and pay zero estate duty. Also, your unused R3.5M rolls over to her, so when SHE dies she has R7M of abatement. This is why most estate planning tries to delay estate duty to the second death.

CGT on Death

The tax man treats your death as if you "sold" everything at market value — even if nothing was actually sold. This triggers capital gains tax on the growth since you bought each asset. BUT if you leave things to your spouse, she "takes over" your original purchase price (no tax yet — just deferred). For anything going to kids, friends, etc., your estate must pay the CGT before distributing. The annual exclusion in the year you die is R440,000 (much bigger than the R50,000 you get while alive) and your primary residence is shielded up to R3 million of gain.

Life Insurance in Estate Planning

Most people's estates are "asset rich but cash poor" — you own a house, shares, and a business, but you don't have a million rand in the bank. When you die, the estate owes taxes and fees immediately. Without cash, the executor may have to sell the family home to pay SARS. Life insurance is the answer — it pays out cash quickly. But who you name as the beneficiary matters a LOT. Naming your spouse is usually the best: the payout avoids executor's fees (saving 3.99%), doesn't count in the accrual, and qualifies for the s4(q) deduction. Naming your estate means the money is available to pay estate costs directly — but you give up 3.99% to executor's fees.

Trusts

A trust is a legal "box" that holds assets for someone else's benefit. Three roles: the founder (who sets it up and puts assets in), the trustees (who manage the assets — like a board of directors), and the beneficiaries (who benefit from the trust). Two main types: Inter vivos trusts (set up while you're alive — used to shift growth out of your estate and protect assets from creditors) and testamentary trusts (set up in your will — typically used to hold assets for minor children until they're old enough). Downside: retained income in a trust is taxed at a flat 45%, and kept capital gains at 36% effective — brutal. That's why trustees usually distribute income/gains to beneficiaries who are in lower tax brackets.

Intestate Succession

If you die without a valid will, the Intestate Succession Act decides who inherits. If you have a spouse but no kids, spouse gets everything. If you have kids but no spouse, kids share equally. If you have both, spouse gets the greater of R250,000 or a "child's share", and kids share the rest. If neither, your parents inherit. Since 2021 (Bwanya case), permanent life partners are also now covered — but informal dating partners are NOT. This is why having a will is one of the most basic adult financial tasks.

Maintenance of Surviving Spouse

If you die and leave a spouse who genuinely can't support themselves (e.g. stay-at-home partner), the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act lets them claim "reasonable maintenance" from your estate — even if your will left them nothing. The court looks at the estate's size, the spouse's own means, and their standard of living during the marriage. Same priority as a dependent child's claim. Since 2021 this applies to permanent life partners too.

Usufruct & Bare Dominium

A usufruct is the right to USE property without owning it — like being given the keys to a holiday home for life. Bare dominium is owning property you can't use — you hold the title deed but can't live there. Classic estate planning: a husband dies and leaves his wife a usufruct over the family home (she lives there for life), with his children getting the bare dominium (they own it on paper). When she eventually dies, the children automatically become full owners. This lets the husband provide for his wife AND ultimately transfer the home to his children, without a second estate duty event.

Liquidity Analysis

Every estate must pay bills: funeral, debts, taxes, executor's fees. Liquidity analysis simply asks: does the estate have enough cash (or near-cash) to pay these without having to sell assets in a hurry? If yes — great. If no — you have a "liquidity shortfall" and need to plan for it, usually with life insurance. This is often where clients find out their estate plan is broken.

Retirement Funds on Death

Retirement fund benefits (pension, provident, RA, preservation) are treated completely separately from your estate. They're not subject to estate duty, not subject to executor's fees, not in the accrual. They sit in their own "bubble" and pass directly to your nominated beneficiary (or as the trustees decide under s37C). This makes retirement savings one of the most tax-efficient ways to pass wealth on death. Catch: withdrawals are taxed at the death lump sum tax table (first R550,000 tax-free).

Buy-and-Sell Agreements

If you own a business with partners and you die, there's a problem: your heirs inherit your shares (they want cash, not shares in a business they don't know how to run), and your partners are now in business with your widow or kids. A buy-and-sell agreement solves this: each partner takes out a life policy on the others' lives. When one dies, the survivors get insurance payouts and use them to buy the deceased's shares from the estate at a pre-agreed price. Everyone wins: the family gets cash, the business continues smoothly, and if structured properly (partners own the policies, not the deceased), the insurance proceeds are NOT included in the deceased's estate for estate duty.

The Mental Model to Keep in Mind

When you die, three "taxes" can be triggered:
  1. Income tax on any income earned up to your date of death (final return).
  2. CGT on the "deemed disposal" of all your assets at market value (s9HA).
  3. Estate duty on your net estate minus the R3.5M abatement.
And three "costs":
  1. Executor's fees (3.99% including VAT).
  2. Master's fees (up to R7,000).
  3. Debts & funeral (whatever was outstanding).
The estate plan's job is to minimize these (via spouse bequests, trusts, retirement structuring, insurance) and make sure there's enough cash to pay them without fire-selling assets.

Personal Risk & Life Stages Planning ▼

The Concept of Risk

In financial planning, risk is the possibility that something will happen that causes a financial loss — death, disability, dread disease, loss of income, damage to assets, liability claims, etc. Risk management is about identifying, measuring, and addressing these risks before they occur.

Six-Step Risk Management Process

  1. Identify the risks (what could go wrong?).
  2. Measure the risks (how likely? how big?).
  3. Select risk treatment (avoid, reduce, retain, transfer).
  4. Implement the plan (take out insurance, draft will, etc.).
  5. Monitor and review.
  6. Adjust as circumstances change.

Four Ways to Treat Risk

  • Avoid: Don't engage in the risky activity (e.g. don't skydive).
  • Reduce: Take steps to lower likelihood or impact (e.g. alarm system, health check-ups).
  • Retain (self-insure): Accept the risk and pay if it happens (e.g. small excess on car insurance).
  • Transfer (insure): Pass the financial consequences to an insurer in exchange for a premium. This is the core of most financial planning.

Six-Step Financial Planning Process

  1. Establish the professional relationship (engagement letter).
  2. Gather client data & define goals.
  3. Analyse financial status.
  4. Develop & present recommendations.
  5. Implement recommendations.
  6. Monitor & review.

Key Financial Ratios

Solvency ratio = Total Assets / Total Liabilities
   (should be > 1 — you own more than you owe)

Liquidity ratio = Liquid Assets / Monthly Expenses
   (should be 3–6 months of expenses)

Debt-service ratio = Monthly Debt Payments / Gross Income
   (should be < 36%)

Savings ratio = Savings / Gross Income
   (target: 10–20% of gross income)

Effective vs Nominal Interest Rate

  • Nominal rate: The stated annual rate (e.g. 12% p.a. compounded monthly).
  • Effective rate: The true annual rate accounting for compounding. For 12% p.a. compounded monthly: (1 + 0.12/12)^12 − 1 = 12.68%.
  • Why it matters: Always compare loans on effective rate. A loan at "15% nominal compounded monthly" costs more than "15.5% effective annual".

Insurance Overview (Short-Term & Long-Term) Planning ▼

Short-Term Insurance

Covers assets and liabilities — things that can be damaged, stolen, or cause you legal liability. Premiums are paid monthly/annually and cover applies for the contract period (typically 1 year). Governed by the Short-Term Insurance Act 53 of 1998 and FSCA regulations.

Main Short-Term Products

  • Motor insurance: Comprehensive (own damage + third party), third-party only, or third-party fire & theft.
  • Homeowners (buildings): Required by most bond-providers. Covers damage to the structure.
  • Household contents: Covers movables inside the home (furniture, appliances, clothing).
  • All-risks: Portable/valuable items — laptops, cameras, jewellery, taken outside the home.
  • Personal liability: Protection against legal claims for damage caused to others.
  • Business / commercial: Professional indemnity, public liability, business interruption.

Key Short-Term Insurance Concepts

  • Indemnity principle: Insurance puts you back where you were — no profit from a loss.
  • Average: If you're under-insured, the insurer pays proportionally. E.g. R500K cover on a R1M house = insurer pays only 50% of any partial loss.
  • Excess: The portion of each claim you pay yourself. Higher excess = lower premium.
  • Utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei): You must disclose all material information. Non-disclosure can void the policy.
  • Insurable interest: You can only insure something you stand to lose financially.

Long-Term Insurance

Covers life-related events — death, disability, dread disease, retirement. Contracts run for years or life. Governed by the Long-Term Insurance Act 52 of 1998.

Disability Cover: Own vs Reasonable Occupation

  • Own occupation: Pays if you can't do YOUR specific job (e.g. a surgeon unable to operate). More expensive. Preferred for specialists.
  • Reasonable occupation: Pays only if you can't do any job reasonably suited to your training/experience. Cheaper. The insurer may argue you can retrain.

Dread Disease Benefit Structures

  • Stand-alone: Separate cover; full amount paid on qualifying diagnosis.
  • Accelerator: Attached to life cover; reduces the life cover amount by whatever is paid on dread disease.
  • Rider benefit: Additional benefit on a main policy; does not reduce main cover.

Key Long-Term Insurance Act Sections

  • s44: Statutory prescribed information the insurer must give.
  • s45: Policyholder protection rules.
  • s47: Limitations on accepting premiums from third parties.
  • s49: Rules on cooling-off period (31 days on most policies).
  • s51: Minor beneficiaries — how proceeds are paid/held.
  • s54: Assumption of rights — assignment/cession.
  • s55: Assistance policies (R30,000 max benefit).
  • s63: Insolvency protection (policy 3+ years, life = policyholder or spouse; assets purchased with proceeds protected for 5 years).

Policyholder Protection Rules (PPR) 2017

The PPR 2017 replaced the older rules and apply to all long-term insurance policies. Key rules:

  • Rule 7 — Advertising: Advertisements must be clear, fair and not misleading. Specific rules on product comparisons.
  • Rule 11.5 — Replacement of policies: When an adviser replaces one policy with another, they must complete a replacement form and show the client is not worse off. Prevents "churning" for commissions.
  • Rule 15A — Complaints handling: Insurers must have a documented complaints process, respond within set timeframes, and escalate to the Ombudsman if unresolved.
  • Rule 21 — Intermediary services: Requires upfront disclosure of commissions, fees, conflicts of interest.
Note: The PPR is basically consumer protection for insurance. It stops shady advisers pushing you to cancel your old policy just so they can earn new commission on a replacement, forces clear disclosure of fees, and gives you a way to complain if things go wrong.

The Five-Fund Tax Approach (for endowments & sinking funds)

When you invest in an endowment or sinking fund, the insurer is taxed on the investment returns — not you. The insurer divides its investment pools into five funds, each taxed at a different rate depending on who the policyholder is:

(1) Endowments are tax-efficient for clients in the 45% marginal bracket (because 30% inside the policy is cheaper than 45% in their own hands) but can be tax-inefficient for clients earning under R500,000 (they'd pay less than 30% directly).
(2) After 5 years, endowments can be surrendered tax-free in the client's hands because the tax was already paid inside the fund.

Capital Needs Analysis (CNA) Calculation ▼

What is a CNA?

A capital needs analysis calculates how much capital (money) is needed to provide for someone's needs on a specific event — most commonly death of a breadwinner or disability. The output tells you how much life/disability insurance is required. This is the core calculation engine of financial planning.

5-Step CNA Process

  1. Identify the need: Income replacement for spouse? Children's education? Pay off home loan? Estate liquidity?
  2. Calculate the capital required to meet the need (Present Value of a growing annuity for income needs; face value for lump-sum needs).
  3. Identify capital available (existing investments, life cover, retirement benefits, etc.).
  4. Calculate the shortfall (Required − Available).
  5. Recommend how to fund the shortfall (new life cover, additional savings, restructuring).

A/B/C/D Template

A = Immediate cash needs (lump sums due at death)
  Funeral costs
  Outstanding debts (home loan, credit cards)
  Estate duty, CGT, executor's fees, Master's fees
  Education fund for children (may be lump-summed)
  Emergency fund for surviving family

B = Ongoing income needs (income replacement)
  Calculate PV of growing annuity = PMT / (r − g) × [1 − ((1+g)/(1+r))^n]
  Where PMT = annual income need, r = after-tax real return, g = income growth, n = years needed

C = Capital already available
  Existing life insurance payable to family
  Cash & investments
  Retirement fund benefits (if payable to spouse/children)
  Other income-producing assets

D = Shortfall = (A + B) − C
  This is the amount of new life cover required.

Real Rate of Return (Essential for Step B)

Real Rate = (Nominal Rate − Inflation) / (1 + Inflation)

Example: If nominal return = 10%, inflation = 6%:
Real Rate = (0.10 − 0.06) / (1 + 0.06) = 3.77%

Use the real rate when inflating the income need each year is built into the calculation (growing annuity).

Worked CNA Example

Scenario: A husband dies. Spouse (age 40) needs R30,000/month (R360,000 p.a.) income for 40 years. After-tax nominal return: 9%. Inflation: 6%.

Step 1 — Real rate: (0.09 − 0.06) / 1.06 = 2.83%
Step 2 — PV of 40-year annuity at 2.83%: Using a financial calculator (BEGIN mode, PMT = R360,000, i = 2.83, n = 40): PV ≈ R8,640,000

Add: lump sum needs (A):
  Home loan settlement: R1,500,000
  Education fund: R800,000
  Estate costs (duty, CGT, fees): R400,000
  Total A = R2,700,000

Deduct: capital available (C):
  Existing life cover payable to spouse: R3,000,000
  Investments: R1,500,000
  Total C = R4,500,000

Shortfall (D) = (A + B) − C = (R2.7M + R8.64M) − R4.5M = R6,840,000
Recommendation: Additional R6.84M life cover, payable to spouse as nominated beneficiary to avoid executor's fees and qualify for s4(q) deduction.

1. Marital Regimes and Their Estate Impact Law ▼
Note: Marriage regimes answer ONE question: "When this marriage ends (death or divorce), whose stuff is whose?" COP says "all one pot." OCP no accrual says "everything separate, always." OCP with accrual says "separate during the marriage, but share the growth at the end."

In Community of Property (COP)

Practical context: This is the default in South Africa — if you get married without signing an antenuptial contract (ANC) before the wedding, you are automatically married in community of property. Many couples are COP without realising the implications.

  • Joint estate; all assets and liabilities shared 50/50.
  • Both spouses equally liable for debts of the joint estate — even debts incurred by only one spouse.
  • On death, half the joint estate belongs to surviving spouse and does NOT form part of the deceased estate.
  • The deceased's half is distributed according to will or intestacy.
  • Practical impact: The surviving spouse's half is automatically protected — creditors of the estate cannot touch it. However, the downside is that the surviving spouse is also liable for the deceased's debts during the marriage.
  • Estate planning implication: Because only 50% of the joint estate forms the deceased estate, the estate duty exposure is automatically halved, which can be an advantage for estate duty planning.

Section 15(2)(c) — COP Spousal Consent Requirements

In a COP marriage, the Matrimonial Property Act requires written consent from both spouses for certain major transactions. You CANNOT act alone on:

  • Alienating (selling / donating) immovable property from the joint estate — e.g. you can't sell the family home without your spouse's signature.
  • Mortgaging immovable property — bond applications need both signatures.
  • Standing surety / suretyship for another person's debts.
  • Entering credit agreements (big loans).
  • Withdrawing from deposit accounts held in spouse's name.
  • Alienating investments, shares, or insurance policies that form part of joint estate (but see Naidoo v Discovery Life — pure risk policies are different).
Note: In a COP marriage, big financial decisions need BOTH spouses' signatures. Think of it like a joint bank account where both names are on everything — one person can't move the big stuff alone. This protects one spouse from the other making reckless decisions. Third parties (like banks) have a duty to verify marital status before accepting a signature (Marais NO v Maposa).

Out of Community of Property (No Accrual)

Practical context: This is the most "separate" marital regime. Typically chosen when both spouses have significant assets or businesses they want to keep entirely separate, or where one spouse has significant debts/risk exposure (e.g. an entrepreneur).

  • Completely separate estates; no sharing on death or divorce.
  • Each spouse retains their own assets and liabilities.
  • Requires an antenuptial contract (ANC) signed before the marriage and registered within 3 months.
  • Risk for non-earning spouse: A stay-at-home spouse who sacrificed a career has no automatic claim to any growth in the other spouse's estate. This is why the accrual system was introduced — to address this unfairness.

Out of Community of Property WITH Accrual

Practical context: This is the most commonly chosen regime for couples signing an ANC. It offers the best of both worlds: independence during the marriage (separate estates, no shared liability) but fair sharing of growth on dissolution.

The accrual system shares the growth that occurred during the marriage. It only applies on dissolution (death or divorce).

Accrual Claim = (Larger Accrual - Smaller Accrual) / 2

Where: Accrual = Current Net Value - Commencement Value (adjusted by CPI)

Commencement Values

  • Declared in the ANC at date of marriage.
  • Adjusted by CPI (Consumer Price Index) to date of dissolution.
  • If not declared, the commencement value is nil (i.e. zero) — this means ALL net assets at dissolution are treated as growth during the marriage.
Scenario example: John and Sarah marry with accrual. John declares a commencement value of R500,000 and Sarah declares R100,000. At John's death 20 years later, after CPI adjustment John's commencement value is R1.2M and Sarah's is R240,000. John's net estate is R8M (accrual = R8M - R1.2M = R6.8M). Sarah's net estate is R2M (accrual = R2M - R240,000 = R1.76M). Claim = (R6.8M - R1.76M) / 2 = R2.52M payable to Sarah. This R2.52M is deductible from John's estate under s4(1A) for estate duty.

Exclusions from Accrual

  • Assets specifically excluded in the ANC.
  • Inheritances, donations, and legacies received during the marriage (unless ANC states otherwise).
  • Non-patrimonial damages (e.g. pain and suffering).
  • Donations between spouses.

  • At death: Rolls over to spouse — s9HA(2)(b) ITA.
  • At divorce: Rolls over — Par 67, Eighth Schedule.
Key exam point: The accrual claim is a deduction under s4(1A) for estate duty purposes. The spouse with the smaller accrual has a claim against the estate of the deceased.

Other Recognised Marriages & Unions

South African law recognises multiple forms of marriage and relationship beyond the standard civil marriage. Each has distinct estate planning implications.

Customary Marriages (Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998)

  • Concluded according to the customs of a particular community (e.g. lobola negotiations, handover ceremony).
  • Must be registered within 3 months (but non-registration doesn't invalidate the marriage).
  • Default regime: In community of property, unless an ANC was concluded.
  • Sithole v Sithole 2021 ZACC 7: Constitutional Court declared all customary marriages concluded before 2 December 1988 are automatically in community of property (previously they were OCP). This is retrospective — it applies to all existing customary marriages.
  • Moropane v Southon 2014 SCA: Set out jurisdictional requirements under s3(1) RCMA for a valid customary marriage.
  • Polygamous customary marriages: Permitted. Each wife has equal rights in estate, maintenance, succession. Estate Duty Act s4A(3) provides for abatement sharing across multiple spouses.

Civil Unions (Civil Union Act 17 of 2006)

  • Applies to both same-sex and opposite-sex couples who register under the Act.
  • Can be described as a "marriage" or "civil partnership".
  • Default regime: Same as civil marriages — COP unless ANC.
  • Parties regarded as spouses for: Matrimonial Property Act, Estate Duty Act, Income Tax Act, Intestate Succession Act, Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act. Full spousal rights for s4(q) deduction, etc.

Religious Marriages (Muslim, Hindu)

  • Religious marriages (e.g. Muslim nikah) were historically not fully recognised under civil law.
  • Daniels v Campbell 2003: Widow under Muslim marriage recognised as "spouse" for Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act.
  • Hassam v Jacobs 2009 CC: Polygamous Muslim marriages recognised for Intestate Succession Act.
  • Moosa NO v Harneker 2017: "Surviving spouse" in Wills Act s2C(1) includes spouse in polygamous Muslim marriage.
  • Parties to a Muslim or Hindu marriage are now treated as spouses for estate duty (s4(q)) and succession purposes.

Cohabitation / Life Partnerships (Post-Bwanya)

  • Bwanya v Master of the High Court 2021 ZACC 51: Landmark Constitutional Court judgment. A permanent opposite-sex life partner is now recognised as a "spouse" for the Intestate Succession Act AND the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act.
  • Gory v Kolver 2007 CC and Laubscher NO v Duplan 2016 ZACC 44 previously extended these rights to same-sex life partners.
  • Judicial Matters Amendment Act 15 of 2023 codifies the post-Bwanya position.
  • Important limitation: The partnership must be "permanent". Evidence required: shared household, cohabitation period, financial interdependence, public representation as a couple.
  • Still NOT recognised for: Matrimonial Property Act (no automatic property regime); accrual system (doesn't apply). Life partners should still consider a cohabitation agreement, wills, and nominations.

Key Case Law for Marital Property

  • E v E [2019] ZAKZPHC 40: Commencement values in ANC are only prima facie proof — the actual commencement value can be proved differently in dispute. Overturned Olivier v Olivier.
  • M v M 2020 SCA: On divorce, the capital in a living annuity is NOT property for accrual purposes, BUT the future annuity income stream IS valued and included.
  • Naidoo v Discovery Life 2018 SCA: A pure risk life policy with a nominated beneficiary is not an asset of the joint estate during lifetime — s15(2)(c) spousal consent is NOT required to change the beneficiary nomination in a COP marriage.
  • Marais NO v Maposa 2020 SCA: Concerning s15(2)(c) consent for alienation — the third party has a duty of enquiry to establish marital status before accepting a large transfer/donation.

2. Estate Duty Calculation (2026/2027) Tax ▼

Governing Act: Estate Duty Act 45 of 1955. The Act uses the terms "total property" (property + deemed property), "net value of estate", and "dutiable estate" — align with this terminology in exam answers.

Note: Estate duty is like a "final toll" on your way out of the financial world. You add up everything you owned (plus some things treated as if you owned them, like insurance). You subtract your debts, costs, and anything left to your spouse. The first R3.5M is free. You pay 20% on the next bit, and 25% on anything crazy big (over R30M). The whole aim of estate planning is to keep the amount you pay small — usually by leaving lots to your spouse (who is fully exempt) or using trusts to shift growth out of your estate during your lifetime.

The 5-Step Estate Duty Calculation (Textbook Method)

Step 1: Value all property s3(2)
Step 2: Add all deemed property s3(3) = Total property
Step 3: Less allowable deductions s4 = Net value of estate
Step 4: Less s4A abatement (R3,500,000) = Dutiable estate
Step 5: Apply rate: 20% on first R30M; 25% above R30M = Estate duty

Step-by-Step Estate Duty Calculation (Detailed)

Step 1: Property in Estate (s3(2))

All assets owned by the deceased at date of death, valued at market value.

  • Immovable property, investments, bank accounts, vehicles, household effects.
  • Business interests, share portfolios, loan accounts.
  • COP note: If married in community of property, only the deceased's 50% share of the joint estate is included here.
  • Practical tip: Loan accounts owed to the deceased (e.g. a loan to a family trust) are assets in the estate even though the cash isn't in the bank — this often catches people off guard in estate duty calculations.

Step 2: Deemed Property (s3(3))

Practical context: "Deemed property" isn't actually owned by the deceased but SARS includes it anyway. The logic is to prevent people from avoiding estate duty by structuring assets outside their estate.

  • s3(3)(a): Domestic policy proceeds on the deceased's life (regardless of who the beneficiary is). Includes policies with nominated beneficiaries. Even if the proceeds go directly to a child or spouse and never touch the estate, it is still deemed property for estate duty.
  • s3(3)(b): Donatio mortis causa — a gift made "in contemplation of death" that only takes effect on death.
  • s3(3)(c): An accrual claim in favour of the deceased (e.g. where the surviving spouse has the larger accrual and owes the deceased's estate).
  • s3(3)(d): Property the deceased was "competent to dispose of" immediately before death — e.g. certain assets in a trust where the deceased retained effective control.
  • s3(3)(e): Disallowed retirement fund contributions — contributions made post-1 March 2016 that were NOT deducted for income tax. This targets voluntary, non-deductible "top-up" contributions — SARS views these as savings disguised inside a retirement fund.
  • Domestic policies on a third party's life owned by the deceased fall under s3(2) (property, not deemed property) — e.g. if you own a policy on your business partner's life, you own the policy as an asset.

Exclusions from s3(3)(a) — Policies NOT Deemed Property

  • s3(3)(a)(i) — ANC/Postnuptial policies: A policy where the proceeds are payable to the surviving spouse in terms of an ANC or postnuptial contract is excluded.
  • s3(3)(a)(iA) — Buy-and-sell policies: Excluded if ALL three requirements met: (1) policy taken out by a co-owner/partner; (2) purpose is to purchase deceased's interest; (3) deceased paid no premiums.
  • s3(3)(a)(ii) — Keyperson policies: Excluded if ALL four requirements met: (1) not effected by or at the instance of the deceased; (2) no premium paid by the deceased; (3) no amount payable to the deceased's estate; (4) no amount payable to a relative, dependant, or family company of the deceased.
Premium recovery (domestic policies): Where a domestic policy is deemed property but someone else actually paid the premiums, the deemed property amount is reduced by (premiums paid × (1.06)^years) — i.e. premiums compounded at 6% per year. This prevents double-counting where a third party funded the policy.

Step 3: Total Property = Property + Deemed Property

Step 4: Allowable Deductions (s4)

The Estate Duty Act s4 lists specific deductions. Key ones:

  • s4(a): Funeral, tombstone, and deathbed expenses.
  • s4(b): Debts owed at date of death to SA residents. Includes outstanding income tax and CGT liability.
  • s4(c): Administration costs — executor's fee (3.5% + 15% VAT = 4.025% effective; commonly rounded to 3.99%) and Master's fee.
  • s4(f): Debts owed to non-residents.
  • s4(p): Reduction in valuation of unlisted shares where deemed property (e.g. buy-and-sell proceeds) increased the share value. Prevents double-taxation. Example: A 40% shareholder dies with a R9M keyperson policy owned by the company on his life. The company receives R9M, which increases the value of his 40% shareholding by 40% × R9M = R3.6M. Without s4(p), that R3.6M would be taxed twice (once as deemed property from the policy, once as increased share value). s4(p) removes the R3.6M from the share value to prevent this double-hit.
  • s4(1A): Accrual claim payable to surviving spouse. Deductible from the estate before applying s4(q).
  • s4(q) — Spouse deduction: Property accruing to the surviving spouse:
    • Bequests to spouse (under will or intestate).
    • Residue going to spouse.
    • Life insurance payable to spouse (even though deemed property).
    • Note: Property must be included in the estate to qualify. Retirement fund benefits to a spouse do NOT get s4(q) — they aren't property anyway.
    • Disallowed where: property is onward-disposed to a third party/trust; spouse has only a limited interest (e.g. usufruct) for less than full value.

s4(q) Limitations (Figure 5.2 in textbook)

s4(q) deduction is REDUCED where:
  • The spouse has only a limited interest (e.g. usufruct) — only the value of that limited interest is deductible, not the full property value.
  • The spouse is obliged to onward-dispose to a third party or trust whose beneficiaries are not the spouse.
  • Property passes to a testamentary trust where beneficiaries other than the spouse can benefit.
s4(q) deduction is DISALLOWED where:
  • The deduction has already been allowed under another s4 provision.
  • The property represents disallowed retirement fund contributions (s3(3)(e)) — these are "fictitious" property, not real inheritance.

Step 5: Net Estate = Gross Estate - Deductions

Step 6: Abatement (s4A)

Abatement: R3,500,000 per person
Rolled-over abatement: If first-dying spouse's abatement was not fully used, the balance rolls over to surviving spouse (s4A(2)).
Maximum for second-dying: R7,000,000
Why the rollover matters: If the first-dying spouse leaves everything to the surviving spouse, the entire estate is deducted via s4(q) — the R3.5M abatement is unused because the dutiable amount is already zero. That unused R3.5M rolls to the surviving spouse, giving them up to R7M abatement on second death. This is a powerful planning tool: the first estate pays zero duty, and the second estate has double the abatement.

Step 7: Dutiable Amount = Net Estate - Abatement

Step 8: Estate Duty

20% on the first R30,000,000
25% on everything above R30,000,000

Apportionment of Estate Duty (s11)

Estate duty is generally paid from the residue of the estate. However, under s11 of the Estate Duty Act, if deemed property (e.g. life insurance) goes directly to a beneficiary (bypassing the residue), the estate can recover a proportional share of the duty from that beneficiary.

Apportionment formula:
Estate Duty recoverable from beneficiary = (Deemed property portion ÷ Net value of estate) × Total estate duty

Practical point: The will can override this — it can specify that estate duty is paid from the residue ("free from duty"), which means non-residue beneficiaries are spared. Most wills do this for simplicity.

Residency Rules (s4A + s3 of Estate Duty Act)

  • SA residents: Estate duty on worldwide assets.
  • Non-residents: Estate duty on SA-situs assets only (e.g. SA property, SA shares).
  • Non-residents do NOT get the full R3.5M abatement — they get a reduced abatement.

Valuation for Estate Duty Purposes (s5)

  • Fair market value at date of death (arm's length).
  • Bona fide farming property: Valued at 70% of market value (i.e. a 30% reduction).
  • Unlisted shares (s5(1)(f)bis): Specific factors to prevent manipulation — always valued at market value, ignoring any buy-and-sell price if lower.
  • Usufruct on property: Annual value = 12% of property value × life expectancy factor.
  • Limited rights ceasing on death: Value = zero (the right dies with the holder).
Common exam trap: Life insurance payable to a nominated beneficiary is deemed property (s3(3)(a)) and included in total property, BUT if the beneficiary is the surviving spouse, it qualifies for the s4(q) deduction, effectively removing it from the dutiable estate.

3. Capital Gains Tax on Death (2026/2027) Tax ▼

Deemed Disposal on Death

Section 9HA of the Income Tax Act deems all assets disposed of at market value on date of death.

Practical context: When you die, SARS treats it as if you "sold" everything at market value on the day you died — even though nothing was actually sold. This triggers capital gains tax on the difference between what you paid (base cost) and what it's worth at death. The estate must settle this CGT bill before assets can be distributed to heirs.

Note: Picture it like this — on the day you die, SARS takes a photograph of everything you own and "pretends" you just sold it all. They calculate the profit you'd have made (market value minus what you paid for it). A chunk of that profit is added to your final tax return. Your estate pays the tax out of your assets before anything goes to your heirs. The only exception: anything you leave to your spouse is "pushed forward" — no tax now, but your spouse inherits your original base cost so she pays the CGT if she ever sells or when she dies.

Roll-over to Surviving Spouse

  • Assets inherited by or bequeathed to the surviving spouse roll over at base cost.
  • No CGT is triggered on these assets at first death.
  • The surviving spouse inherits the original base cost.
Key concept — deferred, not avoided: The rollover does NOT eliminate CGT. It defers it to the second death (or whenever the spouse eventually disposes of the asset). The surviving spouse inherits the original base cost, so the full gain from original purchase to final disposal is taxed eventually. This is important in a scenario — leaving everything to a spouse eliminates CGT at first death but creates a potentially larger CGT bill at second death.

Non-Spouse Heirs

  • CGT is triggered (deemed disposal at market value).
  • Capital gain = Market value at death - Base cost.
  • The estate is liable for the CGT; this CGT liability becomes a deduction for estate duty.
  • Practical point: The heirs inherit the asset at its market value on date of death as their new base cost — so they are not taxed again on the same growth if they later sell.

Exclusions from CGT

  • Personal-use assets (vehicles, furniture, household goods): excluded.
  • Retirement fund benefits: not subject to CGT.
  • Loan bequeathed to trust: No CGT if the loan forms property in the estate for estate duty purposes (Par 12A, Eighth Schedule).
CGT on Death Calculation:
1. Capital gain = MV at death - Base cost
2. Less: Annual exclusion (R440,000)
3. Taxable capital gain = Net gain x 40% (inclusion rate)
4. Tax = Taxable capital gain x marginal rate (max 45%)
5. Effective maximum rate: 40% x 45% = 18%
Primary residence exclusion (R3,000,000): Only available to natural persons or special trust type (a). Not available to ordinary trusts. Applied before the annual exclusion.

4. Life Insurance in Estate Planning Planning ▼

Why Life Insurance Matters in Estate Planning

The core problem: When someone dies, the estate needs cash immediately — to pay executor's fees, estate duty, CGT, debts, and funeral costs. But most estates are "asset-rich, cash-poor" (e.g. property, shares, business interests). Life insurance provides instant liquidity so that heirs don't have to sell assets at fire-sale prices to settle the estate.

Note: Imagine your entire net worth is a R10M house, R5M in shares, and R100K in the bank. You die. The taxman wants R2M. Where does that R2M come from? Your family can't eat the house and they can't pay SARS with shares. Someone has to sell assets — fast, often at a discount. Life insurance solves this: for a relatively small monthly premium, a large cash lump sum appears exactly when it's needed. That's why planners obsess over beneficiary nominations — it's the difference between "family gets R2M immediately, tax-efficiently" and "family loses the house to a forced sale."

The planning question is always: Who should be the beneficiary? This single decision affects executor's fees, estate duty, accrual, and how quickly the money is available.

Example: If proceeds go to the estate (no beneficiary), the money is available to pay estate costs but attracts executor's fees (3.99%) AND is included in the accrual. If proceeds go to the spouse as nominated beneficiary, it bypasses the estate (no executor's fees, not in accrual), is still deemed property for estate duty but immediately offset by s4(q). The spouse gets the money fast and the estate duty impact is nil. This is why nominating the spouse is almost always the best option when the goal is liquidity for the surviving family.

Buy-and-Sell Agreement Policies

Policies taken out between co-shareholders. If properly structured (owned by co-shareholders), proceeds are exempt from estate duty in the deceased's estate.

Why "properly structured" matters: The key is who owns the policy. If shareholder A owns a policy on shareholder B's life, when B dies the proceeds are paid to A (not to B's estate). Since A owns the policy and receives the proceeds, it is NOT deemed property in B's estate under s3(3)(a) — s3(3)(a) only applies to policies on the deceased's life where the deceased is the policyholder, or to any policy on the deceased's life regardless of ownership. However, in a properly structured buy-and-sell, the proceeds never enter the deceased's estate because the surviving shareholder receives them directly and uses them to purchase the shares from the estate at fair value.

What Happens if NOT Properly Structured

  • If the company owns the policies instead of the co-shareholders, the proceeds go to the company — not the surviving shareholder. The company then has cash but no obligation to buy the shares, and the estate is left holding illiquid shares.
  • If a shareholder owns a policy on their own life, the proceeds form part of their estate (deemed property under s3(3)(a)), attract executor's fees, and increase estate duty.
  • Bottom line: The agreement must ensure each shareholder owns a policy on the other shareholder's life, and the agreement obliges the survivor to buy and the estate to sell.

Section 63: Long-Term Insurance Act

  • Insolvency protection: Proceeds protected if:
    • Policy in force for 3+ years
    • Life assured is the policyholder or their spouse
  • Assets purchased with protected proceeds are protected for 5 years.
  • Exception: Fraud/intent to defraud creditors.
Planning tip: A policy payable to the estate attracts both executor's fees AND is included in accrual. Nominate a beneficiary (preferably spouse) to avoid executor's fees and accrual inclusion. If spouse is beneficiary, s4(q) deduction eliminates estate duty impact.

5. Trusts — Types and Taxation Law ▼
Note: A trust is like opening a "safe" that you put assets into for the benefit of others. Once assets go in, you don't own them anymore — the trust does. Trustees (like custodians of the safe) manage the assets, and beneficiaries (your kids, your grandchildren) receive the value. Trusts are used for three main reasons: (1) Estate freezing — future growth happens inside the trust, not in your estate, reducing future estate duty; (2) Asset protection — trust assets are harder for creditors to touch; (3) Long-term planning — assets can pass through generations without going through each person's estate. The main catch: trust tax is harsh (45% flat on retained income, 36% effective on retained capital gains). The key strategy is always to distribute income to lower-taxed beneficiaries.

Trust Basics — The Three Roles (Layman's Terms)

Before getting into trust types, understand the three roles that make up every trust:

RoleWhat They Do (in plain English)
Founder (donor / settlor)The person who creates the trust and puts assets into it. Like the "owner" of the idea. Once assets go in, they're no longer the founder's — they're the trust's.
TrusteesThe people who manage the trust — like a board of directors. They hold legal title to trust assets. Have a "fiduciary duty" (strict legal duty of care) to act in the beneficiaries' interests. Most trusts have 2–4 trustees; at least one should ideally be independent.
BeneficiariesThe people who benefit from the trust — they receive income or capital distributions. Trust deeds usually name a class (e.g. "my children and their descendants").
Analogy: Think of a trust like a company, but for family wealth. The founder is like the founder of a company. The trustees are like the board of directors — they make the decisions. The beneficiaries are like shareholders — they get the value. And like a company, the trust is a separate legal "thing" — the assets inside belong to the trust, not to any individual.

Vested vs Discretionary Trusts

  • Vested trust: The beneficiaries have a fixed, guaranteed right to the income/capital (e.g. "50% to my son, 50% to my daughter on my death"). Trustees just execute — no discretion.
  • Discretionary trust: Trustees have the power to decide WHO gets WHAT and WHEN within a named class (e.g. "my spouse, children, and grandchildren"). This is the most common type of family trust in SA — it gives flexibility as family circumstances change.

Who Regulates Trusts?

  • Master of the High Court: Supervises. Issues Letters of Authority to trustees (can't act without these). Can investigate and remove trustees who fail in their duties.
  • Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988: Sets the legal framework for trust administration.
  • SARS: Taxes trusts and enforces anti-avoidance rules (s7, s7C, s25B).

When and Why Each Trust Is Used

Understanding when to recommend each trust type is as important as knowing the tax rates.

Inter Vivos Trust (Living Trust)

Created during the founder's lifetime by donating or selling assets to the trust. Commonly used for:

  • Estate freezing: Transfer growth assets (e.g. shares, property) into the trust so future growth happens outside your estate, reducing future estate duty.
  • Asset protection: Assets in the trust are owned by the trust (a separate legal entity), offering some protection from personal creditors — though this is limited if the transfer was done to defraud creditors.
  • Continuity: The trust doesn't "die" — assets pass seamlessly between generations without going through the winding-up process of a deceased estate.
  • Downside: Punitive tax rates (45% flat income tax, 80% CGT inclusion = effective 36% CGT) if income/gains are retained. Plus s7C deems interest-free loans as donations.

Testamentary Trust

Created in the deceased's will and only comes into existence on death. The most common reason:

  • Minor children: If a parent dies and the children are under 18, you cannot leave assets directly to minors. A testamentary trust holds and manages the assets until the children reach a specified age (often 18 or 25 as the will may specify).
  • Protecting vulnerable beneficiaries: Even adult children who are financially irresponsible or at risk of divorce — the trust protects the inheritance from being dissipated or claimed by an ex-spouse.
  • Controlling distribution: The will can set conditions (e.g. "income distributed for education until age 25, then capital distributed in thirds at ages 25, 30, and 35").
  • Tax rate: Same punitive 45% flat rate as inter vivos trusts — UNLESS it qualifies as a Special Trust (a) or (b).

Special Trust Type (a) — Disabled Persons

  • Created for a person with a mental or physical disability that is serious and continuous, preventing them from earning sufficient income or managing their own affairs.
  • Gets the best tax treatment of any trust: individual rates, 40% CGT inclusion, R50,000 annual exclusion, AND the R3,000,000 primary residence exclusion.
  • Practical point: The beneficiary must be the sole beneficiary of the trust. If other beneficiaries are added, it loses special trust status.

Special Trust Type (b) — Minor Children

  • A testamentary trust (created in a will) where the youngest beneficiary is under 18.
  • Gets individual tax rates for income (not 45% flat) — a significant advantage.
  • BUT: Does NOT get the R50,000 annual CGT exclusion or primary residence exclusion.
  • Critical timing: The trust automatically loses its special status and becomes an ordinary trust (45% flat rate) when the youngest beneficiary turns 18. This has major tax planning implications — assets may need to be distributed or restructured before this date.
Exam scenario tip: If a question involves parents with young children who die, the testamentary trust for minors is almost always the recommended structure. It protects the children's inheritance, provides managed distribution, and qualifies for favourable Special Trust (b) tax rates until the youngest child turns 18. Always check the ages of the children in the scenario — if the youngest is 16, the special trust benefit only lasts 2 years.

Section 25B — Conduit Principle

Practical context: The conduit principle is the main tool for managing trust tax. Because trusts are taxed at 45% flat on retained income, the strategy is to distribute income to beneficiaries who are in lower tax brackets. The trust acts as a "pipe" — income flows through it to beneficiaries and is taxed in their hands.

  • Income distributed to beneficiaries is taxed in the beneficiary's hands.
  • Income retains its nature (interest remains interest, rental remains rental, etc.) — so a beneficiary can still claim the interest exemption (R23,800 under 65 / R34,500 over 65) on interest distributed from the trust.
  • Income retained in trust is taxed at 45% flat rate.
Scenario example: A trust earns R200,000 rental income. If retained in the trust: taxed at 45% = R90,000 tax. If distributed to a beneficiary earning R150,000 per year: the R200,000 is added to their taxable income and taxed at their marginal rate (likely 26-31%), saving significant tax. The trustee's decision on whether to distribute or retain has major tax consequences.

Capital Gains in Trusts

  • Vested in beneficiary: Taxed in beneficiary's hands at individual rates (40% inclusion, R50,000 exclusion).
  • Retained in trust: 80% inclusion rate, 45% tax rate (effective 36%).
  • Unless attributed to donor under Par 70/72.
  • Key distinction: Capital gains can only be "vested" in a beneficiary if the beneficiary has a vested right to the capital of the trust. In a discretionary trust (the most common type), the trustees decide — beneficiaries have no automatic vested right, so capital gains are usually taxed in the trust at 36% effective rate.

Section 7C — Interest-Free/Low-Interest Loans

Why this exists: Before s7C (introduced 2017), the most popular estate planning strategy was to sell assets to a trust funded by an interest-free loan. The assets (and their growth) moved out of the individual's estate, the loan was repaid over time, and no donations tax applied because it was a sale not a donation. SARS closed this loophole with s7C.

  • Loan to trust at less than the official interest rate = deemed donation.
  • Deemed donation = (Official rate - Charged rate) x Outstanding loan balance / 12 per month.
  • Annual donations tax exemption of R100,000 applies.
  • Donations tax: 20% on first R30M cumulative; 25% above.
Scenario example: A founder has a R10M interest-free loan to a family trust. The official rate is (say) 7.75%. The deemed donation per year = 7.75% x R10M = R775,000. After the R100,000 annual exemption, donations tax of 20% applies to R675,000 = R135,000 per year in donations tax. This is the ongoing "cost" of maintaining the interest-free loan structure. The planner must weigh this cost against the estate duty savings of keeping the growth assets out of the estate.
Planning response to s7C: Many planners now advise either (a) charging the official interest rate on the loan (which eliminates the deemed donation but means the interest income is taxable in the founder's hands), or (b) gradually donating the loan away using the R100,000 annual exemption to reduce the outstanding balance over time.

Par 70/72 Eighth Schedule

Capital gains from trust assets (originally donated/transferred at non-arm's length) may be attributed back to the donor and taxed in the donor's hands at individual rates.

Practical impact: This means even if you've moved assets into a trust, the capital gains on those assets may still be taxed as if YOU sold them. The trust doesn't shield you from CGT on donated assets — it's an anti-avoidance measure to prevent people from shifting gains to a trust simply to defer or reduce CGT.

6. Intestate Succession (Act 81 of 1987) Law ▼

Practical context: Intestate succession applies when someone dies without a valid will (or where the will doesn't deal with certain assets). In South Africa, a surprising number of people die intestate. The Intestate Succession Act then dictates exactly who inherits what — the deceased has no say. This is why having a valid, up-to-date will is one of the most basic estate planning recommendations.

Note: Think of the Intestate Succession Act as the "default settings" the government applies if you don't bother writing a will. These defaults are rigid and often don't match what people actually want. E.g. defaults assume you want your kids to share equally — but you might have wanted more for the child with special needs. Defaults don't know about your business partner, your charity, your stepson. So "dying intestate" means the state writes your will for you, using generic rules that probably don't fit your life.

Distribution Rules

SurvivorsDistribution
Spouse only, no descendants Spouse inherits everything
Descendants only, no spouse Descendants inherit per stirpes
Spouse + descendants Spouse gets greater of a child's share or R250,000 (Gazette amount); descendants get residue
No spouse, no descendants Parents inherit equally. If one parent predeceased: surviving parent gets 50%, deceased parent's descendants get other 50%
Child's share = Estate value / (number of children who survived or predeceased leaving descendants + 1 for the spouse)

Important Definitions

  • "Spouse" now includes permanent life partners (Judicial Matters Amendment Act 15 of 2023).
  • Adopted children are deemed descendants of adoptive parents.
  • Per stirpes: Descendants of a predeceased child step into that child's place.
Blended families — common exam scenario: Intestate succession does NOT distinguish between children from different marriages. All biological and adopted children have equal claims. If a man has 2 children from a first marriage and 1 from a second marriage, all 3 share equally. The second spouse gets the greater of a child's share or R250,000 — but the first (divorced) spouse gets nothing (they are no longer a "spouse"). This is a key reason to have a will — intestacy rules may not match what the deceased would have wanted, especially in complex family situations.
Scenario example (per stirpes): A father dies intestate with no spouse. He has 3 children: A, B, and C. Child B predeceased the father but left 2 children of their own (grandchildren). Distribution: A gets 1/3, C gets 1/3, and B's 2 grandchildren share B's 1/3 equally (each gets 1/6). The grandchildren "step into" their parent's place.

Section 6 — Renunciation by Descendant

Where a descendant (not a minor or mentally ill person) who, together with the surviving spouse, is entitled to an intestate benefit, renounces their right to inherit, that benefit vests in the surviving spouse. This is a useful estate planning tool — e.g. if adult children are financially independent, they may renounce so the entire estate passes to the surviving parent (avoiding duty and simplifying administration).

Wills & Testate Succession (Wills Act 7 of 1953) Law ▼

Practical context: A will allows you to decide who inherits your assets, choose your executor, nominate guardians for minor children, and set up testamentary trusts. Dying with a valid will is called "testate succession" and is governed by the Wills Act.

Requirements for a Valid Will (s2 Wills Act)

  • Testator must be 16 years or older and mentally capable of understanding the nature and effect of the will.
  • Must be in writing (typed or handwritten).
  • Must be signed by the testator at the end (and each preceding page).
  • Must be signed in the presence of two competent witnesses, each 14+ years old, who both sign in each other's and the testator's presence.
  • Witnesses must NOT be beneficiaries (or the will is invalid as to their bequests).

Section 2(3) — Power of Court to Condone Defects

If a will does not meet all formal requirements (e.g. a page not signed, missing witness signature), the court CAN declare it valid if satisfied the document was intended by the testator to be their will. This is an important safety valve — but not a substitute for proper execution.

Section 2B — The "Ex-Spouse Rule" (Deemed Divorce)

  • If a testator divorces and dies within 3 months of the divorce, any bequest to the ex-spouse in a will made before the divorce is treated as if the ex-spouse had predeceased the testator.
  • Policy rationale: Gives the testator 3 months to update their will after divorce. If they don't, the law assumes they WOULD have updated it.
  • After 3 months: The bequest stands — the ex-spouse inherits.
  • Louw NO v Kock 2017 WCC: Strict application — even where the court result is harsh, s2B applies mechanically.

Section 2C — Substitution of Descendants

  • s2C(1) — Renunciation: If a descendant (who was to inherit together with the surviving spouse) renounces their bequest, that benefit vests in the surviving spouse. Parallel to s6 Intestate Succession Act.
  • s2C(2) — Predeceased descendant: If a beneficiary descendant predeceases the testator, THEIR descendants inherit per stirpes. E.g. if "my child Ann" was to inherit R1M but Ann died before me leaving 2 children, those 2 grandchildren split R1M equally.

Common Will Provisions

  • Specific bequests: "R500,000 to my nephew"; "my car to my sister".
  • Residuary bequest: "The rest of my estate to my spouse".
  • Testamentary trust: "If my children are under 25 at my death, their inheritance is to be held in trust until they reach 25".
  • Guardianship nomination: "I nominate [X] as guardian of my minor children".
  • Executor appointment: "I appoint [Y] as executor, free from security".
  • Attestation clause: Standard closing statement.

Freedom of Testation & Its Limits

South Africa has broad freedom of testation — you can leave your estate to whomever you choose. However, limits exist:

  • Maintenance claims: Surviving spouse and dependent children can claim maintenance even if disinherited.
  • Public policy / unconstitutional provisions: A bequest that is racially discriminatory or offends public policy can be struck down (e.g. Curators ad litem v King Edward VII Trust).
  • Matrimonial property: A spouse in COP cannot bequeath MORE than their half-share of the joint estate.
Planning tip: A will should be reviewed after every major life event — marriage, divorce, birth of children, death of a beneficiary, acquisition of significant new assets, or starting a business. An outdated will can cause disastrous unintended consequences.

Redistribution Agreements Between Heirs

Practical context: Sometimes what the will says doesn't work for the heirs in practice. E.g. Dad's will gives the family farm to all 3 children equally — but only one child wants to farm. The other two prefer cash. Heirs can voluntarily enter into a redistribution agreement to "swap" inheritances, as long as ALL heirs agree.

How It Works

  • All heirs must consent and sign the agreement.
  • Assets/bequests are reshuffled (e.g. farming child gets the farm; others get cash from the estate or a loan from the farming child).
  • The redistribution is treated as if the deceased's will had always said that — so no CGT is triggered by the reshuffle (assets still come from deceased estate).
  • For estate duty, the reshuffle is also generally neutral — the same assets are included in the estate at the same values.
  • Rollover relief for CGT still applies if assets end up with the surviving spouse.
Note: Redistribution agreements let the family say, "Dad's will was good in principle, but we'd prefer to rearrange things amongst ourselves." As long as everyone agrees, and no new tax events are triggered, it's a flexible way to make an inheritance work for the real family circumstances.
Important limitation: Redistribution cannot involve third parties (e.g. can't redistribute to a cousin who wasn't in the will) — that would be a donation, not a redistribution. Also, if consideration (money) flows between heirs, there may be CGT consequences for those payments.

7. Maintenance of Surviving Spouse (Act 27 of 1990) Law ▼

Practical context: This Act is a safety net. It protects a surviving spouse who was financially dependent on the deceased — typically a spouse who didn't work or earned very little. The claim arises against the estate before distribution to heirs and can override the will. Even if the deceased left everything to someone else, the surviving spouse can claim maintenance.

Key Provisions

  • Surviving spouse can claim reasonable maintenance from the deceased estate.
  • Only if the spouse cannot provide from own means.
  • Same priority as maintenance of a dependent child.
  • "Spouse" now includes permanent life partners (2023 amendment).
  • Important: This claim exists regardless of the marital regime — even a spouse married out of community of property (no accrual) who would otherwise receive nothing can claim maintenance.

Factors Considered

  • Amount available in the estate.
  • Spouse's own means, earning capacity, and needs.
  • Standard of living during the marriage.
  • Age of surviving spouse.
Scenario example: A husband dies and leaves his entire estate to his children from a first marriage. His second wife (aged 62, no income, married OCP no accrual) would receive nothing under the will and has no accrual claim. However, she can claim reasonable maintenance under this Act. The court would consider that she is 62, has no earning capacity, and was accustomed to a comfortable lifestyle. The maintenance claim has the same priority as the children's claims — the estate must provide for her before the children's bequests are fulfilled.
Estate planning implication: A planner should ensure the estate plan adequately provides for the surviving spouse to avoid a contested maintenance claim that could delay the winding up of the estate. Life insurance payable to the spouse, a usufruct over the family home, or a trust providing an income are all planning tools that address this.

8. Valuation of Limited Interests Calculation ▼

Practical context: A usufruct is one of the most common estate planning tools in South Africa. The classic scenario: a parent dies and wants the surviving spouse to live in the family home for the rest of their life, but ultimately wants the property to go to the children. The will grants the spouse a usufruct (right to live in and use the property) and the children receive the bare dominium (they own it but can't use it until the usufruct ends). When the spouse dies, the usufruct falls away and the children become full owners automatically — no further estate administration needed.

Key Concepts

  • Usufruct: Right to use and enjoy property (and its fruits/income). The usufructuary does not own the property but can live in it, rent it out and keep the rental income, etc.
  • Bare dominium (nuda proprietas): Ownership minus the usufruct. The bare dominium holder owns the property on paper but cannot use it or benefit from it until the usufruct ends.
  • Fiduciary interest: Similar to a usufruct in calculation. A fideicommissum is where property is left to person A (fiduciary) with the instruction that on a certain event (often A's death) it passes to person B (fideicommissary).
  • Habitation (habitatio): A more limited right than usufruct — the right to live in a property but NOT to let it out or benefit from its fruits. Less common but still examinable.
Usufruct vs Fideicommissum — What's the Difference?
Students often confuse these. Here's the clear distinction:

Usufruct: Two parties — the usufructuary (user) and the bare dominium holder (owner). Two different people at the same time. The usufructuary uses; the owner owns. Ends when usufruct period ends (usually usufructuary's death).

Fideicommissum: Two people in sequence — the fiduciary gets full ownership FIRST, then on a trigger event (usually their death) ownership passes to the fideicommissary. It's like a "ownership relay race" — full ownership is transferred through people over time.

Example: "I leave my farm to my son for his lifetime, then to my grandson." If that's a USUFRUCT — son uses the farm, grandson already owns it. If it's a FIDEICOMMISSUM — son fully owns the farm first, then on son's death grandson takes full ownership. Different tax consequences too.

Fideicommissum Substitution Rules

  • If the fiduciary dies BEFORE the fideicommissary: Property passes to fideicommissary as intended. Fiduciary's estate has NO rights to the property.
  • If the fideicommissary dies BEFORE the fiduciary: The fideicommissary's rights (which were only a spes or "hope") fall away — they never vested. Fiduciary keeps full ownership. On fiduciary's eventual death, the property forms part of fiduciary's estate (no longer subject to fideicommissum).
  • Planning tip: A fideicommissum is relatively rigid — once set up, the fiduciary can't sell or alienate the property (under common law) without special consent. Trusts are more flexible and have largely replaced fideicommissa in modern practice.

6-Step Valuation Process

  1. Calculate the annual value: 12% of the market value of the underlying property.
  2. Identify the beneficiary (usufructuary / fideicommissary).
  3. Determine life expectancy using age-next-birthday and Table A.
  4. Is there a fixed period? If so, use Table B for that period.
  5. Use the SHORTER of life expectancy (step 3) and fixed period (step 4).
  6. Capitalize: Multiply annual value by the factor from the applicable table.

When a usufruct ceases (e.g. on death of usufructuary), the gain on the bare dominium cannot exceed:

Maximum gain = Current Market Value - Bare Dominium value when first acquired
Age-next-birthday: For table lookups, always use the age the person will turn on their next birthday, not their current age. E.g. if someone is currently 64 and their birthday is in 3 months, their age-next-birthday is 65.

Estate Duty Implications of Usufruct

  • At first death: The usufruct value is deductible under s4(q) if it accrues to the surviving spouse (it is "property accruing to the spouse"). The bare dominium bequeathed to children is NOT deductible — this portion is dutiable.
  • At second death (usufruct ceases): The usufruct had value in the surviving spouse's estate but it ceases on death (value = zero), so there is nothing to include in the estate for estate duty. The children automatically become full owners without any estate duty.
  • CGT at second death: When the usufruct ceases, the bare dominium holder is deemed to have disposed of and reacquired the usufruct portion. The gain is limited by the second proviso (cannot exceed market value minus original bare dominium cost).
Common exam scenario: A will grants a usufruct to the surviving spouse over a property worth R5M. You must calculate: (1) the usufruct value using 12% x R5M x table factor, (2) the bare dominium as R5M minus usufruct value, (3) the s4(q) deduction for the usufruct (spouse benefit), and (4) estate duty on the bare dominium portion (going to non-spouse heirs). Always check the age of the spouse (use age-next-birthday) and whether the usufruct is for life or a fixed period.

Massing of Estates & Rebate on Rapid Succession Law ▼

Massing of Estates

Practical context: "Massing" is where two or more people (typically spouses) combine their estates into one "massed estate" through a joint will. On the death of the first-dying, the survivor has a choice to either accept (adiate) the arrangement and receive a limited interest (usually a usufruct or life interest in the whole massed estate), OR refuse (repudiate) and take only their own half.

Adiation vs Repudiation

ScenarioEffect
Survivor adiatesAccepts the massed estate terms. Own half is "donated" to the massed estate in exchange for a limited interest. Survivor becomes a usufructuary; children receive bare dominium.
Survivor repudiatesRefuses massing. Keeps own half outright. Only the first-dying's half is distributed per the joint will.

Section 58 Income Tax Act — Deemed Donation on Adiation

Important tax consequence: If the surviving spouse adiates, s58 Income Tax Act may deem the portion of their own estate "donated" to the massed estate as a donation — which could trigger donations tax (20%/25%) if the value of the limited interest they receive is less than the value of what they give up.

  • Deemed donation = Value of property forfeited − Value of limited interest received.
  • If the limited interest is worth MORE than or equal to what is given up, no deemed donation.
  • Spouse-to-spouse donations are exempt from donations tax (s56(1)(c)), so in typical spousal massing this may not be a concern — but always check.

When is Massing Used?

  • Common in older joint wills between spouses.
  • Purpose: provide for surviving spouse's lifetime needs AND ensure assets eventually pass to chosen heirs (usually children).
  • Modern planning generally prefers life insurance + testamentary trusts over massing, because massing can cause complex tax issues and limits the survivor's flexibility.

Rebate on Rapid Succession (s4A Estate Duty Act)

Purpose: Prevents double estate duty on the same property within a short period. Where property inherited from a first-dying estate is included in the second-dying's estate within 10 years, a rebate is available against the second estate duty.

7-Step Rebate Calculation

  1. Identify the property inherited from first estate that is still in second estate.
  2. Calculate the portion of first estate's duty attributable to that property.
  3. Calculate the portion of second estate's duty attributable to that same property.
  4. Take the lower of (2) and (3).
  5. Apply the time-based reduction scale (see below).
  6. The reduced amount is the rebate.
  7. Deduct rebate from second estate's duty.

Time-Based Reduction Scale

Important limitation: The rebate is NOT available where the first-dying's estate did not actually pay estate duty (e.g. because it was fully offset by s4(q) spouse deduction). There must have been actual estate duty paid for there to be anything to "rebate".

9. Liquidity Analysis Planning ▼

Why this matters: Liquidity analysis is often the final step in an estate planning question. You've calculated the estate duty, CGT, executor's fees — now the question is: does the estate have enough cash to pay all of this? If not, the executor may have to sell assets (possibly the family home or business shares) at unfavourable prices to raise cash. This is the problem life insurance is designed to solve.

Cash Available

  • Money market and bank balances in estate.
  • Life insurance payable to the estate (note: only policies payable to the estate count as cash available to the estate. Policies with nominated beneficiaries bypass the estate).
  • Sinking fund policies.
  • Note: Life insurance payable to the spouse as nominated beneficiary does NOT provide cash to the estate — it goes directly to the spouse. If the estate needs liquidity, the spouse would have to voluntarily contribute those funds back. This is an important planning consideration.

Cash Needed

  • Executor's fees (3.99% incl VAT).
  • Funeral costs (typically estimated at R15,000–R50,000 in scenarios).
  • Outstanding liabilities and debts (home loan, credit cards, personal loans).
  • CGT on death.
  • Estate duty.
  • Cash legacies (specific cash amounts bequeathed in the will).
  • Master's fees.
  • Accrual claim payable to surviving spouse (if applicable).
Shortfall = Cash Needed - Cash Available
(If positive, estate has a liquidity problem)
Exam approach: In a scenario question, always do the liquidity analysis even if not explicitly asked. If there is a shortfall, recommend a solution. If there is a surplus, state that the estate is liquid. This shows thorough analysis and often carries marks.

Grossing-Up the Cover Amount (Critical Exam Technique)

The problem: If you add new life cover payable to the estate to address a liquidity shortfall, that new cover ITSELF becomes part of the estate — so it attracts executor's fees AND potentially more estate duty. You must "gross up" the cover to account for this.

With policy to estate, you need R651K to cover the same shortfall — 30% more premium cost. This is the exam-critical reason spouse nomination beats estate-as-beneficiary in most liquidity scenarios.

Strategies to Address Shortfall

  • Life insurance payable to estate: Provides direct cash to the estate. Downside: attracts executor's fees (3.99%) and is included in the accrual calculation.
  • Life insurance payable to spouse: Avoids executor's fees and accrual; deductible under s4(q) for estate duty. The spouse then informally contributes cash to help the estate. Best option when spouse is the main beneficiary anyway.
  • Surviving spouse contributes from own assets (if married OCP) — but this requires the spouse to actually have liquid assets.
  • Trust or company insures the life of the individual to repay outstanding loans owed to the trust/company.
  • Restructure the estate: Reduce estate duty by leaving more to the spouse (s4(q)), use trusts to move growth assets out of the estate, or restructure business interests via buy-and-sell agreements.

10. Retirement Fund Benefits on Death Planning ▼

Practical context: Retirement fund benefits are treated completely differently from other assets on death. They sit outside the estate in their own "bubble" — not subject to estate duty, not subject to executor's fees, not in the accrual. This makes them one of the most tax-efficient wealth transfer vehicles on death. The key question is always: what happens to the money and how is it taxed when it reaches the beneficiary?

Key Principles

  • Not property in the estate (s37C, Pension Funds Act).
  • Not subject to executor's fees.
  • Not subject to estate duty (except s3(3)(d) for non-deducted contributions post-2016).
  • Not included in the accrual calculation.
  • Why s3(3)(d) exists: SARS wants to prevent people from making extra voluntary contributions to their retirement fund purely to avoid estate duty. If you contribute R500,000 that you didn't deduct for income tax, SARS deems that R500,000 as part of your estate for estate duty purposes — you can't hide money from estate duty by stuffing it into a retirement fund.

Beneficiary Options

Practical context: The beneficiary typically has a choice of how to receive the benefit. The tax consequences differ significantly, and a financial planner should advise on the optimal split.

11. Buy-and-Sell Agreements Planning ▼

The problem buy-and-sell solves: Imagine two people co-own a business 50/50. One dies. Without an agreement, the deceased's 50% share goes to their heirs (spouse, children). The surviving shareholder is now in business with people who may have no interest in or ability to run the business. The heirs want cash, but the share is illiquid. The business may fail. A buy-and-sell agreement solves this by pre-arranging the sale of the deceased's shares to the survivor, funded by life insurance so there is immediate cash available.

Purpose

  • Ensure business continuity upon death of a shareholder/member — the surviving shareholder(s) keep control.
  • Guarantee fair value for the deceased's estate — the heirs receive cash instead of an illiquid business interest.
  • Prevent unwanted parties from inheriting business interests — protects both the business and the family.

How It Works (Step by Step)

  1. Agreement signed: Shareholders agree that on death, the survivor MUST buy and the estate MUST sell the deceased's shares at a pre-agreed or formula-based value.
  2. Policies taken out: Each shareholder takes out a life policy on the other shareholder's life. Shareholder A owns a policy on B's life; B owns a policy on A's life.
  3. Death occurs: Shareholder A dies. B receives the insurance proceeds (because B owns the policy on A's life).
  4. Purchase: B uses the insurance proceeds to buy A's shares from A's estate at the agreed value.
  5. Result: B now owns 100% of the business. A's estate has cash instead of illiquid shares. The business continues without disruption.

Estate Duty Treatment

ItemTreatment in Deceased's Estate
Insurance proceeds (policy owned by survivor) NOT in deceased's estate — paid to the surviving shareholder. Not deemed property because it's not a policy on the deceased's life owned by the deceased.
Business shares Included in deceased's estate at market value as property under s3(2). However, the shares are sold to the survivor, so the estate receives cash to settle liabilities.
Sale proceeds received by estate The estate now has cash (from the sale of shares) instead of shares. This cash is available to pay estate duty, CGT, executor's fees, etc.
Key insight: The buy-and-sell agreement doesn't eliminate estate duty on the business shares — the shares are still in the estate at market value. What it does is ensure the estate has liquidity (cash from the sale) to pay the estate duty and other costs. Without it, the executor might have to sell the shares to a third party at a discount, or the business might have to be liquidated.

Mandatory vs Option Agreement

  • Mandatory (binding): Survivor MUST buy, estate MUST sell. This is the standard and preferred structure. Both parties are bound, providing certainty.
  • Option: Survivor has the OPTION to buy but is not obliged. Less certain — if the survivor chooses not to buy, the estate is stuck with illiquid shares. Not recommended.

Key Requirements

  • MOI alignment: The company's Memorandum of Incorporation must not contain provisions that conflict with the buy-and-sell (e.g. pre-emptive rights, restrictions on share transfers).
  • Valuation method: Must be agreed upon upfront — typically net asset value, earnings multiple, or independent valuation. Disputes over value can delay the entire process.
  • Regular review: Policy values must be kept in line with business value. If the business has grown significantly, the policies may be underinsured — the survivor won't have enough cash to buy at fair value.
  • Premium payment: Each shareholder pays the premiums on the policy they own (the one on the other's life). Premiums are NOT tax-deductible.

Key Person Insurance

Separate concept — do not confuse with buy-and-sell. Key person insurance is taken out by the company on a key employee's life. If that employee dies, the proceeds compensate the company for the financial loss (lost revenue, cost of finding a replacement, etc.). The proceeds go to the company, not to the estate or shareholders.

  • Premiums: paid by the company, NOT tax-deductible (capital in nature).
  • Proceeds: received by the company, NOT taxable (capital receipt) — unless the policy has a cession or surrender value component.
  • Not deemed property in any individual's estate (company owns the policy).
Exam scenario tip: If a question involves business partners, look for: (1) Is there a buy-and-sell agreement? If not, recommend one. (2) Who owns the policies? If the company owns them, flag that this is incorrectly structured. (3) Are the policy values adequate? Compare to business value. (4) Is there a separate key person policy for the company's protection? These are distinct needs requiring distinct policies.

Key South African Court Cases Law ▼

South African estate planning is heavily shaped by Constitutional Court, Supreme Court of Appeal, and High Court decisions. Each case below includes facts (what happened), significance (why it matters), and practical implications (how it affects planning today).

How to use these in an exam: Mention the case name + year + one-line principle. e.g. "Per Bwanya v Master 2021 ZACC 51, permanent life partners now qualify as 'spouse' for intestate succession." That's often worth ½ to 1 full mark per case cited correctly.

Marriage & Life Partnership Cases

Bwanya v Master of the High Court [2021] ZACC 51

Facts: Ms Bwanya was in a long-term committed relationship with Mr Ruch (they lived together, shared finances, were engaged to marry). He died intestate. The Intestate Succession Act at the time only recognised married "spouses" and didn't cover unmarried life partners. Ms Bwanya had no legal claim on his estate despite their partnership. She also couldn't claim maintenance under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act.

What the Constitutional Court decided: Sections of the Intestate Succession Act and Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act were unconstitutional insofar as they excluded permanent opposite-sex life partners. The Court extended the definition of "spouse" to include permanent opposite-sex life partners (same-sex life partners were already covered by earlier cases).

Why it matters: Before Bwanya, if you lived with a partner for 30 years without marrying and died intestate, they got nothing. Now they have rights equivalent to a married spouse for intestate succession AND maintenance. This is a landmark ruling that reshaped modern estate planning for unmarried couples.

Practical limit: Life partners still do NOT have rights under the Matrimonial Property Act (no accrual system). They should still draft wills, cohabitation agreements, and nominations.

Codified by: Judicial Matters Amendment Act 15 of 2023.

Sithole v Sithole [2021] ZACC 7

Facts: Prior to the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act (RCMA) 1998, customary marriages concluded before 2 December 1988 were deemed to be OUT OF community of property by default (section 7(1) RCMA). Mrs Sithole challenged this provision as unfairly discriminatory — spouses in customary marriages had less protection than civil marriage spouses.

Decision: The Constitutional Court declared s7(1) unconstitutional. All customary marriages — WHENEVER concluded — are now deemed in community of property by default, unless excluded by ANC.

Why it matters: This corrected decades of inequality. Spouses (mostly women) in pre-1988 customary marriages now have automatic 50/50 rights to joint estate assets and liabilities. Applied retrospectively.

Practical implication: When planning an estate for someone in a customary marriage pre-2 Dec 1988, assume COP applies unless specific ANC evidence proves otherwise.

Volks NO v Robinson [2005] CC

Facts: Ms Robinson lived with Mr Shandling as life partners for 16 years without marrying. When he died, she was excluded from the definition of "spouse" under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act and received nothing.

Decision at the time: The Constitutional Court held this was NOT unconstitutional — life partners had chosen not to marry and therefore didn't qualify for the protections afforded to married spouses. Was widely criticised as harsh and outdated.

Current status: Partially overruled by Bwanya 2021. Volks still stands for the general principle that marriage creates legal obligations life partners opted out of — but the specific exclusion from maintenance rights was reversed by Bwanya.

Why you should still know it: Shows the legal journey SA has taken on life partner rights, from denial (Volks 2005) to recognition (Bwanya 2021).

Gory v Kolver 2007 (4) SA 97 (CC)

Facts: Mr Gory was in a permanent same-sex life partnership with Mr Brooks. When Brooks died intestate before the Civil Union Act 2006, Gory was excluded from the Intestate Succession Act definition of "spouse" (only applied to married couples).

Decision: The Constitutional Court ruled that the Intestate Succession Act was unconstitutional to the extent it excluded permanent same-sex life partners. The Act was "read in" to include them.

Why it matters: First major SA case extending succession rights to unmarried partners. Established the principle that equality demands recognition of committed partnerships regardless of marriage status.

Practical point: Confirmed by Laubscher NO v Duplan 2016 — same-sex partners are protected even though they could have married post-2006.

Laubscher NO v Duplan [2016] ZACC 44

Facts: Mr Laubscher (executor) argued that because the Civil Union Act had been enacted in 2006, same-sex couples COULD have married if they wanted — so the court's reading-in from Gory should no longer apply.

Decision: The Constitutional Court disagreed. Not all same-sex partners want to formalise via the Civil Union Act. The intestate succession rights for permanent same-sex life partners (read in from Gory) REMAIN IN FORCE.

Why it matters: Confirms same-sex life partners have intestate rights even if they chose not to marry. Paved the way for Bwanya 2021 extending this to opposite-sex partners.

Moropane v Southon [2014] JOL 32177 (SCA)

Facts: Ms Southon claimed she was married to the deceased per customary rites. The deceased's family disputed this, arguing requirements for a valid customary marriage under RCMA s3(1) weren't met.

Decision: The SCA set out the jurisdictional requirements for a valid customary marriage:

  • Both prospective spouses must be 18+ years old
  • Both must consent to marry each other under customary law
  • Marriage must be negotiated and entered into or celebrated in accordance with customary law
  • (Registration is not a validity requirement, just administrative)

Why it matters: When a customary marriage is disputed, this is the test. All four requirements must be satisfied on a balance of probabilities.

Daniels v Campbell [2003] (Muslim marriage)

Facts: Mrs Daniels was married to her late husband under Muslim rites only (not civil marriage). Her stepchildren excluded her from any share of the estate, arguing she wasn't a "spouse" for Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act purposes.

Decision: The Constitutional Court ruled that "spouse" in the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act INCLUDES widows of Muslim marriages, regardless of whether polygamous.

Why it matters: Started the recognition of religious marriages in SA succession law. Muslim widows now have maintenance claim rights equivalent to civil-marriage spouses.

Hassam v Jacobs [2009] (11) BCLR 1148 (CC)

Facts: Mr Hassam had multiple wives in a polygamous Muslim marriage. When he died intestate, the surviving wives (his widows) tried to claim under the Intestate Succession Act but were excluded because monogamous civil marriage was the only form recognised.

Decision: Constitutional Court extended "spouse" under the Intestate Succession Act to include wives in polygamous Muslim marriages. Multiple wives can each claim as a "spouse".

Why it matters: Recognition of polygamous marriages in SA intestate succession law. Each spouse shares in the intestate distribution. Estate Duty Act s4A(3) also provides for abatement sharing across multiple spouses in polygamous marriages.

Moosa NO v Harneker 2017 (6) SA 425 (WCC)

Facts: Mr Moosa, in a polygamous Muslim marriage, had a will leaving bequests to beneficiaries "together with my spouse". Question was whether his second wife in a polygamous Muslim marriage qualified as "spouse" for Wills Act s2C(1) purposes (the section allowing descendants to renounce in favour of the spouse).

Decision: Yes — "surviving spouse" in Wills Act s2C(1) includes a spouse in a polygamous Muslim marriage.

Why it matters: Extended the polygamous spouse recognition from intestate succession (Hassam) to TESTATE succession (wills). Any polygamous Muslim spouse can benefit under s2C(1) if descendants renounce.

Bhe v Magistrate Khayelitsha [2005] (1) BCLR 1 (CC)

Facts: Mr Bhe died intestate. Under customary law primogeniture, the eldest male relative would inherit — excluding his two minor daughters. The children and their mother challenged this.

Decision: The Constitutional Court struck down customary law male primogeniture as unconstitutional (violated dignity and equality). Ordered that the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987 applies to all South Africans, regardless of race or cultural background.

Why it matters: Transformative judgment ending male-preference succession. Now, the Intestate Succession Act rules apply uniformly across SA. Daughters, sons, and spouses all qualify per the Act's ordinary rules.

Accrual & Matrimonial Property Cases

E v E [2019] ZAKZPHC 40

Facts: In an accrual divorce case, the wife claimed her actual commencement value was higher than the R15,000 recorded in the ANC. She produced evidence of significant assets she owned at marriage (property, savings) totalling much more.

Decision: The KZN High Court held that ANC commencement values are only prima facie proof — they can be rebutted by evidence of the actual commencement value. The spouse bears the onus of proving the actual value differs from the ANC figure.

Why it matters: Changed longstanding position from Olivier v Olivier 1998 (where ANC values were treated as binding). Overturned Olivier. Significant for accrual calculations — if ANC values are low or nil and one spouse has evidence of a much higher actual commencement value, they can ask the court to use the actual value in the accrual calculation.

Exam tip: If a question has a spouse claiming their commencement value differs from the ANC, cite E v E and note the onus of proof is on the spouse asserting the higher value.

M v M [2020] ZASCA 48

Facts: In a divorce, the husband held a large living annuity. The wife argued his living annuity capital should be included in his estate for accrual calculation purposes. The husband argued only the income (not capital) was his.

Decision: The SCA held:

  • The CAPITAL in a living annuity is NOT the annuitant's property for accrual purposes (it's owned by the insurer, annuitant just has a right to income)
  • BUT the right to future annuity income IS a valuable asset and must be valued and included in the annuitant's estate for accrual

Why it matters: Clarifies the accrual treatment of living annuities at divorce. Only the income-stream value (actuarially calculated) enters the accrual — not the capital balance. This softens the impact of accrual for a retired spouse.

Practical tip: At divorce, an actuary must be engaged to value the living annuity income stream. At death, retirement benefits are excluded entirely from accrual (s37C).

Naidoo v Discovery Life [2018] JOL 39960 (SCA)

Facts: Mr Naidoo, married in community of property (COP), took out a pure risk life insurance policy and nominated his mistress (not his wife) as beneficiary. His wife discovered the policy after his death and argued that, per s15(2)(c) of the Matrimonial Property Act, his changing the beneficiary without her consent was invalid — the policy therefore should pay to her (as spouse in COP).

Decision: The SCA held that a pure risk life policy with a nominated beneficiary is NOT an asset of the joint estate during the insured's lifetime (it has no surrender value — nothing to "own" until death). Therefore, s15(2)(c) spousal consent is NOT required to change the beneficiary. The mistress kept the proceeds.

Why it matters: Resolves a major question in COP marriages: can one spouse unilaterally change beneficiaries on pure risk policies? Answer: YES. Only savings products with a surrender value (endowments, sinking funds) would require consent.

Practical point: Planners can advise COP spouses they can change risk policy beneficiaries without the other spouse's consent. This doesn't stop family disputes, but it clarifies the legal position.

Marais NO v Maposa [2020] ZASCA 23

Facts: A husband, married in COP, donated substantial assets to Maposa without his wife's consent (required under s15(2)(c) Matrimonial Property Act). The wife, via the estate, sought to void the donation. Question was whether Maposa should have known spousal consent was required.

Decision: The SCA held that a third party receiving a significant donation from a married person has a DUTY OF ENQUIRY to establish the donor's marital status. If they fail to enquire and take the donation at face value, the donation can be unwound and the party may be required to return the property.

Why it matters: Shifts some of the burden onto the recipient (or their attorney). Banks, purchasers, and donees dealing with married persons in large transactions must verify marital regime and obtain spousal consent.

Wills, Succession & Maintenance Cases

Louw NO v Kock 2017 (3) SA 62 (WCC)

Facts: Mr Kock and his wife Louw divorced. Mr Kock died 3½ months after the divorce, BEFORE updating his will (which still named his ex-wife as sole heir). Under Wills Act s2B, if a testator dies within 3 MONTHS of divorce and hasn't updated the will, the ex-spouse is treated as having predeceased. But here he died just PAST the 3-month mark (by a few weeks).

Decision: The WCC applied s2B strictly — since he died MORE than 3 months after the divorce, s2B did NOT apply. The ex-wife inherited everything per the outdated will. This was harsh given the obvious intent (he would have updated if he'd lived) but the court wouldn't extend the 3-month rule.

Why it matters: Teaches a hard lesson: update your will within 3 months of divorce, or the statutory protection disappears. After 3 months, the law assumes your existing will (with ex-spouse included) reflects your current intentions.

Practical tip: Planners should immediately schedule a will review after any divorce notification.

Oshry NO v Feldman [2010] JOL 25900 (SCA)

Facts: Mrs Feldman (surviving spouse) claimed maintenance from her late husband's estate under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act. The executor disputed the amount she claimed. She wanted continued support at the standard of living she was accustomed to during the marriage.

Decision: The SCA set out factors to consider when determining "reasonable maintenance":

  • Amount available in the estate
  • Existing and expected means of the surviving spouse
  • Earning capacity, financial needs, and obligations
  • Duration of the marriage
  • Standard of living during the marriage
  • Age of the surviving spouse

Why it matters: The leading authority on MSSA maintenance quantification. Whenever you encounter a maintenance claim question, cite Oshry for the factors.

Kruger NO v Goss [2010] 1 All SA 422 (SCA)

Facts: Mr Goss divorced his wife. The divorce settlement agreement ordered him to pay her rehabilitative maintenance (short-term support to help her become self-sufficient). He died before the maintenance period expired. His deceased estate argued the obligation died with him.

Decision: The SCA held that an ex-spouse's rehabilitative maintenance order is NOT automatically enforceable against the deceased estate. It only binds the estate if the settlement agreement explicitly stated it would survive his death.

Why it matters: Important for estate planners drafting divorce settlements. If a maintenance obligation is intended to continue beyond the payer's death, the settlement MUST state this explicitly.

Tax & Estate Duty Cases

Estate Late JM Klosser 63 SATC 93

Facts: Mrs Klosser inherited a usufruct over a substantial share portfolio from her late husband's estate. The portfolio produced actual annual dividend income well below the statutory presumed 12% yield. SARS wanted to value the usufruct using 12% yield; Mrs Klosser argued for the actual (lower) yield.

Decision: The Commissioner accepted that where the actual yield is demonstrably different from 12%, the real yield can be used for usufruct valuation purposes (s5(2) Estate Duty Act permits this).

Why it matters: Authority for using ACTUAL yield in usufruct valuations when 12% doesn't reflect reality. Applies to both estate duty valuations and CGT base cost calculations on ceasing usufructs.

Practical exam note: If a question gives actual yield data AND says "Commissioner accepted [X%] as reasonable", use the given yield, not 12%.

Trust Law Cases (Foundational)

Crookes v Watson 1956 AD

Facts: Trust founders tried to amend the trust deed to remove or alter existing beneficiaries' rights. Beneficiaries argued their rights had already vested and couldn't be changed unilaterally.

Decision: The Appellate Division held that once beneficiaries have ACCEPTED benefits under a trust (even tacitly), their rights are vested via the stipulatio alteri (contract for the benefit of a third party) principle. The trust deed CANNOT be amended to reduce or remove their rights without their consent.

Why it matters: Foundational SA trust law. Once beneficiaries accept, their rights are protected. Relevant for founder-driven amendments and estate planning scenarios where a founder tries to redirect benefits late in life.

Land and Agricultural Bank of SA v Parker 2005 (2) SA 77 (SCA)

Facts: The Parker family trust was used as a personal "piggybank" by the founder — no real separation between his personal affairs and the trust's affairs. Creditors of the founder tried to "pierce the trust veil" and access trust assets to satisfy his debts.

Decision: The SCA established that where a trust is a mere "alter ego" of its founder (no real separation of control, no arm's length dealings, founder treats trust assets as own), courts can DISREGARD the trust as a separate entity. Creditors can then access trust assets as if they were the founder's personal assets.

Why it matters: Warning to planners about "sham trusts". If you set up a trust but continue treating its assets as your own (transferring at will, no proper trustee resolutions, no separate bank account), it may be pierced. Essential for asset protection — trusts only work if properly administered.

Exam Tip — Which Cases To Memorise:

  • Essential foundations: Crookes v Watson, Land Bank v Parker (trusts); Bhe (uniform intestate rules)
  • Useful supporting cases: Daniels, Hassam, Moosa (religious marriages); Oshry (maintenance factors); Louw NO v Kock (s2B strict application)
