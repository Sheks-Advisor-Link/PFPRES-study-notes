Follow-up Questions & Answers

Q1

Question 1 — Liquidity planning

Kevin's estate has a R3,729,558 shortfall. Advise a plan so there's no shortfall. If recommending life insurance, assume premium R18/month per R10,000 cover. Explain taking into account duty/tax the policy may attract.

Memo — Q1

Plan 1: Maria contributes from own assets.

  • CIS (R2,450,000) — risky, not liquid cash, would attract CGT on disposal
  • Money market R350,000
  • Policy proceeds to Maria: R1,000,000
  • She can pay the shortfall into the estate to prevent forced asset sales.

Plan 2: New life policy to Maria (recommended). Residue is bequeathed to Maria. Policy to her is free of executor's fee and qualifies for s4(q) deduction.

Cover needed: R3,729,558
Premium: R3,729,558 × 18 ÷ 10,000 = R6,713/month

Q3

Question 3 — CGT on accrual satisfaction

Maria has a R2,556,471 accrual claim against Kevin's estate. If the estate satisfies the claim by transferring assets, what are the CGT implications?

Memo — Q3

The accrual claim is a cash claim against the estate. If there's insufficient cash, assets can be transferred in settlement.

Those assets qualify for the spouse rollover — s9HA(2)(b) Income Tax Act. Maria inherits Kevin's base cost; no CGT is triggered at first death. Tax is deferred until Maria disposes of the asset or dies.

Q4

Question 4 — Bequeathing loan accounts

Kevin has two credit loan accounts. He's been told NOT to bequeath the trust loan to the trust due to negative CGT implications. Advise.

Memo — Q4

Sistema loan (R250,000): If repaid on death, reduces cash shortfall. Recommend Sistema take out a policy on Kevin's life to fund repayment.

Trust loan (R4,500,000): Two options:

  • Trust takes out a policy on Kevin's life to repay the loan on his death; OR
  • Kevin bequeaths the loan to the trust as a legacy. Paragraph 12A Eighth Schedule — where the loan is bequeathed AND forms property in the estate for estate duty purposes, there is no CGT on the loan "write-off" in the trust's hands.

The advice "don't bequeath" is outdated — Par 12A(6) provides relief.

Q5

Question 5 — Trust income taxation (Grandpa Trust)

  • 5.1 Why is a portion of trust income taxed in Kevin's hands?
  • 5.2 How can trust income avoid being taxed at 45% in the trust?

Memo — Q5

5.1: Income is retained in the trust subject to the trustees exercising their discretion. This triggers s7(5) Income Tax Act — income retained because of a conditional/discretionary disposition is deemed to be the donor's (Kevin's father originally; or Kevin indirectly).

5.2: Trustees must distribute the income to beneficiaries. This triggers the s25B conduit principle — income retains its nature and is taxed in beneficiaries' hands (at their hopefully lower marginal rate). Also allows beneficiaries to use their interest exemption on interest income.

Q6

Question 6 — CGT on trust asset

Grandpa Trust sold an asset for R1,500,000 (base cost R900,000). Advise the trustees how to ensure the gain is taxed at a lower rate.

Memo — Q6

  • Vest the capital gain in one of the discretionary beneficiaries (if the trust deed authorises this).
  • The gain is then taxed in the natural person's hands: 40% inclusion rate (vs. 80% in trust); benefit of annual exclusion (R40,000 / now R50,000); lower marginal rate.
  • Effective CGT: 40% × individual marginal rate vs. 36% flat in trust.
Q7

Question 7 — Testamentary trust for children

Advise Kevin on the type of testamentary trust to use for his children and its tax implications.

Memo — Q7

Use a Special Trust Type (b) — a testamentary trust where the youngest beneficiary is under 18 (Eleanor is 15).

  • Taxed at individual rates for income (not 45% flat).
  • 40% CGT inclusion rate (not 80%).
  • BUT no R50,000 annual CGT exclusion; no primary residence exclusion.
  • Loses special status when youngest beneficiary turns 18 — becomes ordinary trust.

Case Study — Johan & Sarah Classic case study — OCP accrual, blended family, buy-and-sell ▾

Facts

Johan (57) & Sarah (55), OCP WITH accrual (not excluded). ANC didn't declare values = commencement is NIL for both. Johan has 2 kids from previous marriage: Mark (25) and Janet (22) — self-supporting. Sarah has Petunia (14) from previous marriage (first husband died before she married Johan 2005). No maintenance claims.

Johan works at bank + owns 50% of Top Hats (Pty) Ltd (imports from China). Co-shareholder Blaine (35) owns 49%, Mark owns 1%. Johan no salary from company; Blaine runs it full-time. Company reinvests profits. Johan considering early retirement from bank to join Top Hats full-time. Provident fund retirement age 62 (can take early from 55).

Johan's assets (base cost / MV): Primary res R750K/R3.25M | Share portfolio R450K/R1.2M | Foreign shares R300K/R680K | Top Hats 50% R800K/R1.25M | Unit trust R875K/R1.45M | Money market R350K | Motorcar R310K/R250K | Durbanville flat R1.25M/R1.48M. Total MV R9,910,000.

Liabilities: Bond R450K | Car loan R110,107.

Admin (exec excluded) R30K, funeral R20K.

Typical Questions on this case study

Key Planning Issues to Identify

1. Accrual Claim
  • Both commencement values = NIL (not declared in ANC) entire net estate at death is "growth"
  • Sarah's daughter Petunia (14) from her previous marriage — first husband died 2003, so Sarah may have inherited. Any inheritance she received is EXCLUDED from accrual.
  • Mark owns 1% of Top Hats — could impact buy-and-sell structure
2. Buy-and-Sell on Top Hats
  • No existing buy-and-sell mentioned — Johan's 50% is illiquid
  • If Johan dies: Sarah/kids inherit 50% shares but Blaine may not want them in the business; they may not want to run a handbag import company
  • Recommend: Buy-and-sell between Johan, Blaine, Mark. Each owns life policy on other two. s3(3)(a)(iA) exempts proceeds from estate duty
  • Mark's 1% shareholding + plan for Johan to bequeath to Mark on death — align the will with the buy-and-sell
3. Estate Duty
  • Johan's net estate MV R9,350K (after liabilities)
  • s4(q) — whatever residue to Sarah reduces duty
  • Mark's 50% share bequest means that portion is NOT s4(q) dutiable
  • If Mark takes shares, consider special bequest + buy-and-sell proceeds offset
4. CGT on Death
  • Primary residence: R3M exclusion applies (2026/27); rollover if to Sarah
  • Top Hats shares to Mark = CGT triggered. Small business exclusion (R2.7M) if Johan 55+ with business MV < R15M — applies here (his share R1.25M but total business probably around R2.5M)
  • Share portfolio, foreign shares, unit trusts to Sarah rollover. To kids CGT.
5. Retirement planning (taking early retirement from bank at 55)
  • Provident fund: Early retirement from age 55 allowed; value at early retirement = same as at age 62 (per rules)
  • Two-pot implications (from 1 Sep 2024)
  • At retirement: can commute up to 1/3 as lump sum (taxed per retirement lump sum table); 2/3 must buy annuity
  • Living annuity: nominate Sarah as beneficiary; bypasses estate
6. Liquidity
  • Cash in estate: Money market R350K + buy-and-sell proceeds (if structured) + any life cover to estate
  • Cash needed: Funeral R20K + admin R30K + exec fee 3.99% × ~R9.3M = R371K + liabilities R560K + CGT + estate duty + accrual claim to Sarah
  • Likely shortfall — recommend additional life cover to Sarah
7. Estate Duty Minimisation Strategies
  • Leave more to Sarah via s4(q) (defer to 2nd death when abatement is R7M)
  • Buy-and-sell reduces estate duty on business shares
  • Testamentary trust for Petunia (Sarah's minor daughter) if Sarah dies first — Special Trust (b) favourable rates

Albert (52) & Emma (49), OCP EXCLUDING accrual. Daughter Abby (23, self-supporting university lecturer). Albert injured in car accident July 2014, took disability retirement.

  • 2/3 = R6.4M Living annuity; drawdown 2.5% minimum. Current value R6.45M. Emma nominated beneficiary.

Investments (from after-tax lump sum):

  • Money market R360K at 6% p.a.
  • Voluntary purchased annuity R600K (10-yr fixed, R7,929/month, capital tax-free portion R5,000; received 24/120 payments)
  • RSA Retail Savings Bonds R460K at 9% (5 yrs from Aug 2014, paid out to him)

Business: Cameron (Pty) Ltd (advertising). Albert 55%, Mick Lategan (Emma's brother) 45%. Total value R3.5M.

Buy-and-sell: Albert's life policy owned by Mick R1,925,000, Mick pays R1,450/m | Mick's life policy owned by Albert R1,575,000, Albert pays R1,240/m.

Albert's assets: Primary residence R3.2M (BC R2M) | RSA RSBs R460K | Cameron shares R1,925,000 (BC R800K) | Living annuity R6,450,000 | Motor R250K (BC R300K) | Personal use R250K (BC R280K).

Key Exam Issues

Planning Issues

1. Buy-and-Sell Analysis
  • Good structure: Each co-shareholder owns policy on the OTHER's life; each pays own premiums
  • s3(3)(a)(iA) exemption met: not deceased's effect/premiums; purpose = purchase interest; proceeds to co-shareholder
  • BUT: Mick is Emma's BROTHER — is this a "family company" for s3(3)(a)(ii) purposes? Probably not (s3(3)(a)(ii) is keyperson, different test)
  • Cover adequacy: Albert's 55% = R1,925,000 vs shares R1,925,000 matches
  • Mick's 45% = R1,575,000 vs value R1,575,000 matches
2. Living Annuity on Death
  • Emma nominated bypasses estate (not property in Albert's estate)
  • Not deemed property (retirement fund rule s3(2)(i))
  • Options for Emma: lump sum (taxed per retirement lump sum death table, first R550K tax-free); OR continue as own living annuity (drawdowns taxed as income); OR combination.
3. Voluntary Purchased Annuity
  • 10-year fixed term. 24 of 120 payments received. 96 payments remaining.
  • On death: remaining capital forms part of Albert's estate as property
  • Value = PV of remaining payments (can be calculated or agreed with insurer)
  • Capital element (tax-free) portion continues for recipient; interest portion taxable
4. Life Insurance Planning
  • Policy 1 to Emma: s4(q) eliminates estate duty impact; bypasses executor's fees
  • Policy 2 to daughter Abby: still deemed property under s3(3)(a); no s4(q); Abby must contribute to estate duty apportionment
  • Abby is self-supporting 23-yr-old — consider if R1,575,000 is appropriate given her low needs
5. CGT on Death
  • Primary res: R3M exclusion (2026/27), rollover if to Emma
  • Cameron shares to whoever (likely buy-and-sell): gain R1,125,000. Small business exclusion R2.7M available (Albert >55, business <R15M). Result: NO CGT.
  • RSA RSBs: no gain (R460K = R460K)
  • Vehicle, personal use: excluded
  • Money market, CIS: to Emma rollover; to others CGT
6. Estate Duty
  • Property: residence R3.2M, RSBs R460K, Cameron shares R1,925,000 (sold via buy-and-sell = cash R1,925K into estate), living annuity capital NOT property, vehicles R250K, personal R250K, voluntary annuity PV
