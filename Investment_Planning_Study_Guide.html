🧠 Behavioral Finance: The Second Generation

Normal vs. Rational Investors

Standard finance says investors are rational: they only want more wealth, have perfect self-control, and are immune to all cognitive and emotional errors.

Second-generation behavioral finance says investors are normal: they have multiple wants, use shortcuts, and sometimes make errors — but they are NOT "irrational."

Three Types of Benefits from Every Financial Product

  1. Utilitarian: "What does this do for my wallet?"
  2. Expressive: "What does this say about me to others?"
  3. Emotional: "How does this make me feel?"

System 1 vs System 2 Thinking

System 1 (Fast)System 2 (Slow)
Intuitive, automaticDeliberate, analytical
Where shortcuts liveWhere careful analysis happens
Quick decisionsComplex reasoning
Key insight: Shortcuts are NOT always bad! They become errors only when they take people far from their best choice. A shortcut that saves time and gets you 90% there is efficient.

Key Biases to Know

Disposition Effect: Selling winners too early and holding losers too long (driven by pride and regret avoidance).

Mental Accounting: Framing wealth into distinct "buckets" (e.g., "spend dividends but don't dip into capital") as a self-control tool.

Cognitive Biases (errors in thinking) — CAN be fixed through education:

  • Belief perseverance: conservatism, confirmation, representativeness, hindsight, illusion of control
  • Information processing: anchoring, framing, availability, recency, mental accounting

Emotional Biases (based on feelings) — very HARD to change, must ADAPT to them:

  • Loss aversion, overconfidence, self-control, status quo, endowment, regret aversion

γ Alpha, Beta & Gamma (Blanchett & Kaplan)

The Three Greek Letters

ConceptWhat It MeansWho Gets It
AlphaSkill-based return from active managementZero-sum game — hard to get consistently
BetaReturn from systematic market risk (asset allocation)Everyone who is invested
GammaValue from intelligent financial planning decisionsAnyone following an efficient strategy

Gamma = +1.59% annual equivalent alpha (or 22.6% more certainty-equivalent retirement income).

The Five Gamma Factors

  1. Asset Location & Withdrawal Sourcing: Put bonds in tax-deferred accounts, stocks in taxable accounts; withdraw from taxable first
  2. Total Wealth Asset Allocation: Include human capital + Social Security as "bonds" in your total portfolio
  3. Annuity Allocation: Hedge longevity risk with annuities (25% in their model)
  4. Dynamic Withdrawal Strategy: Recalculate annually based on remaining life expectancy — NOT a static 4% rule
  5. Liability-Relative Optimization: Optimise for goals, not just return — hedge inflation and funding risks
Key takeaway: Smart planning decisions (Gamma) are worth MORE than trying to beat the market (Alpha). And unlike Alpha, Gamma is available to everyone.

🎯 Goals-Based Asset Allocation (CFA Institute)

Core Concept: Minimum Expectations vs. Expected Returns

Goals-based investing breaks your portfolio into sub-portfolios, each matched to a specific goal with its own time horizon and required probability of success.

Critical insight: A portfolio with 7% expected return and 10% volatility over 5 years, at 90% confidence, only earns 1.3% per year compounded (not 7%). The correct discount rate for funding goals must account for BOTH time horizon AND required probability.

The Process

  1. Describe all client goals (cash flows, time horizons, required probability)
  2. Match each goal to a pre-optimised portfolio module
  3. Assess funding costs using minimum expectations
  4. Allocate capital to each sub-portfolio
  5. Combine into overall portfolio
  6. Annual review

⚠ Mind the Gap (Morningstar)

The Behavioral Gap: -1.7% Per Year

Morningstar measured what investors ACTUALLY earned vs. what their funds earned over 10 years:

MeasureReturn
Average fund total return7.7% / year
Average investor return6.0% / year
Gap (behavioral drag)-1.7% / year
Why does this happen? Investors buy high and sell low. They chase past performance, panic during downturns, and time the market poorly. This costs them about one-fifth of what they could have earned by simply buying and holding.

Key Findings

  • Allocation funds had the NARROWEST gap (-0.46%) — simple, diversified, easy to stick with
  • Sector equity funds had the WIDEST gap (-4%+) — volatile, narrow, tempting to chase
  • Higher volatility = wider behavioral gap
  • Dollar-cost averaging did NOT significantly help

🔢 The Resultant Rate Method

The Core Formula

Resultant Rate = (Investment Return - Escalation Rate) / (1 + Escalation Rate)
What does this do? When payments grow each year (escalate with inflation), you can't use the normal interest rate in your calculator. The resultant rate strips out the effect of escalation so you can use a simple annuity calculation for growing payments.

The Three-Step Income-Stream Process

StepWhat You DoCalculator ModeRate Used
Step 1Grow today's payment to the future start dateEND modeEscalation rate (e.g., inflation)
Step 2Calculate capital needed at start of income periodBEGIN modeResultant Rate
Step 3Discount that capital back to todayEND modeFull investment return (NOT resultant)
Critical rule: The resultant rate is ONLY used in Step 2 (the income annuity). The accumulation step (Step 3) ALWAYS uses the full investment return rate — never a resultant rate.

Worked Example: R10,000/month for 10 years, escalating at 7%

Return: 8% (years 1-5), 10% (years 6-10). Annual payment = R120,000.

Phase 1 (Years 1-5): BEGIN mode, 8% return

Resultant rate = (8 - 7) / 1.07 = 0.9346%

PMT = 120,000 | N = 5 | I/YR = 0.9346

PV = R588,991

Phase 2 (Years 6-10): Three sub-steps

Step 1: Inflate payment to Year 6: PV=120,000, I/YR=7, N=5 → FV = R168,306

Step 2: Capital at Year 6 (BEGIN): PMT=168,306, N=5, I/YR = (10-7)/1.07 = 2.8037% → PV = R796,864

Step 3: Discount to today (END): FV=796,864, I/YR=10, N=5 → PV = R494,790

Total Capital Needed: R588,991 + R494,790 = R1,083,781

Lump-Sum Capital Need (e.g., Holiday, Car)

Step 1: FV = PV x (1 + inflation)^N   [grow at inflation] Step 2: PV = FV / (1 + return)^N   [discount at investment return]

Example: Holiday in 5 years, current cost R180,000, inflation 7%, return 10%

Step 1: FV = 180,000 x (1.07)^5 = R252,459

Step 2: PV = 252,459 / (1.10)^5 = R156,757

TER Includes

  • Management fees (fixed and performance)
  • Admin costs, custody fees, trustee fees, audit fees, bank charges, taxes
  • Underlying fund expenses for fund-of-funds

TER Excludes: All transaction costs; scrip lending fees earned

EAC Components

EAC[total] = EAC[IMC] + EAC[Advice] + EAC[Admin] + EAC[Other]

TER vs EAC: TER looks BACKWARD (what was spent). EAC looks FORWARD (what will be spent). TER is a component of EAC through the Investment Management Charge.

📈 Measures of Return

Key Return Formulas

Holding Period Return (HPR) = (Capital gain/loss + Income) / Start value
Annual HPR (EAR) = (1 + HPR)^(365/days) - 1

Example: Buy unit trust at R325, sell at R328, receive R3.50 dividend. Held 59 days.

HPR = (3 + 3.50) / 325 = 2%

Annual HPR = (1.02)^(365/59) - 1 = 13.03%

Comparing Different Holding Periods

Fund A: R100 to R150 + R5 dividends over 3 years. Fund B: R200 to R320 + R10 over 4 years.

HPR A = 55% | HPR B = 65%

EAR A = (1.55)^(1/3) - 1 = 15.73% | EAR B = (1.65)^(1/4) - 1 = 13.34%

Fund A wins on an annualised basis despite lower total HPR.

⚠ Measures of Risk

Sharpe Ratio

Sharpe Ratio = (Portfolio return - Risk-free rate) / Standard deviation of portfolio
What it tells you: How much EXTRA return you earned for each unit of TOTAL risk. Higher = better. Negative = you earned less than the risk-free rate.

Other Risk Measures

MeasureWhat It MeasuresInterpretation

💲 Money Market Instruments

Interest Rate vs Discount Instruments

Interest Rate (e.g., NCD)Discount (e.g., Treasury Bill)
How it worksBuy at face value, earn interest on topBuy BELOW face value, redeem AT face value
Rate conventionNACQ (quarterly)NACA (annual)
ExamplesNCDs, short-term debenturesTreasury Bills, Land Bank Bills

NCD (Interest Rate Instrument)

Maturity Value = Amount x [1 + (Days/365) x (Rate/100)]

R1,000,000 NCD, 90 days, 13%:

= 1,000,000 x [1 + (90/365) x 0.13] = R1,032,050

Treasury Bill / Discount Instrument

Price = Face Value - [Face Value x Discount Rate x (Days/365)] Nominal Yield = (Discount / Price) x (365 / Days) EAR = [1 + Yield x (Days/365)]^(365/Days) - 1

TB: Face R100, discount rate 8%, 91 days:

Price = 100 - 100 x 0.08 x 91/365 = R98.01

Nominal Yield = (1.99/98.01) x (365/91) = 8.14%

EAR = [1 + 0.0814 x 91/365]^(365/91) - 1 = 8.39%

FV = face value | PMT = coupon payment | I/YR = current yield | N = years to maturity | PV = price

Annual coupon: R1,000,000 face, 9% coupon, 5 years, 7.5% yield

PMT = 90,000 | FV = 1,000,000 | I = 7.5 | N = 5

Bond Yield Types

Yield TypeFormula
Nominal Yield= Coupon rate
Current Yield= Annual coupon / Market price
Yield to Maturity= IRR of all cash flows (solve for I/YR on calculator)
Rule: When coupon rate > current yield, bond trades at a PREMIUM. When coupon rate < current yield, bond trades at a DISCOUNT.

📉 Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

Gordon Growth Model (Constant Growth)

Share Price = DPS1 / (k - g) where: DPS1 = expected dividend next year k = required rate of return g = constant growth rate

DPS1 = R1.50, k = 12%, g = 5%

Price = 1.50 / (0.12 - 0.05) = R21.43

Two-Stage DDM

V0 = SUM[DPSt/(1+k)^t] + Pn/(1+k)^n Terminal Value: Pn = DPS(n+1) / (k - gn)
Full Two-Stage DDM Worked Example

Given: D1 = R1.20, Year 2 growth = 12%, perpetual growth = 3%, k = 10%

Step 1 — Dividends:

D1 = R1.20 | D2 = 1.20 x 1.12 = R1.344 | D3 = 1.344 x 1.03 = R1.38432

Step 2 — Terminal Value at Year 2:

V2 = 1.38432 / (0.10 - 0.03) = R19.776

Step 3 — Intrinsic Value:

V0 = 1.20/1.1 + (1.344 + 19.776)/1.21

V0 = 1.0909 + 17.4545 = R18.55

Equity Relative Valuation

P/E Ratio = Price per share / EPS Earnings Yield = (EPS / Price) x 100 Dividend Yield = (DPS / Price) x 100
Note: P/E and Earnings Yield are INVERSES of each other. Convert EPS/DPS from cents to rands by dividing by 100.

Key principle: Mismatch resolution is the CLIENT's decision, not the adviser's. The adviser illustrates alternatives and guides — the client decides based on their own values.

Common Risk Profiling Mistakes

  • Ignoring risk capacity entirely (most common single mistake)
  • Relying on interview alone — advisers' estimates correlate at only ~0.4 with actual risk tolerance
  • Couples completing a single questionnaire jointly instead of separately
  • Overweighting time horizon ("longer = safer" is misleading)
  • Using simplistic questionnaires just for compliance
  • Not identifying or resolving mismatches

📑 Master Formula Reference Sheet

Capital Needs Analysis

Resultant Rate = (Investment Return - Escalation) / (1 + Escalation) Pre-tax Income = After-tax Income / (1 - Tax Rate) Three-Step Income Stream: Step 1: PV → FV at escalation rate (grow payment to future date) Step 2: PMT → PV at resultant rate, BEGIN mode (capital at income start) Step 3: FV → PV at investment return, END mode (capital needed today) Lump-Sum Capital Need: Step 1: PV → FV at inflation Step 2: FV → PV at investment return Escalating Investment to Eliminate Shortfall: Step 1: FV → PV at investment return Step 2: PV → PMT at resultant rate, BEGIN mode

Returns & Risk

HPR = (Capital Gain + Income) / Start Value Annual HPR = (1 + HPR)^(365/days) - 1 Sharpe Ratio = (Return - Rf) / Std Dev

Money Market

NCD Maturity Value = Amount x [1 + (Days/365) x Rate] TB Price = Face Value - [Face Value x Discount Rate x (Days/365)] TB Nominal Yield = (Discount / Price) x (365 / Days) TB EAR = [1 + Yield x (Days/365)]^(365/Days) - 1

Bonds & Equities

Bond Price: FV, PMT (coupon), I (yield), N → solve PV Current Yield = Annual Coupon / Market Price YTM: solve for I/YR given PV, PMT, FV, N Gordon Growth: P = DPS1 / (k - g) Two-Stage DDM: V0 = Σ[DPSt/(1+k)^t] + [DPS(n+1)/(k-g)] / (1+k)^n P/E = Price / EPS Earnings Yield = EPS / Price Dividend Yield = DPS / Price

Fund Analysis

Units Purchased = Capital Invested / NAV per unit Annual Income = Units x CPU (cents per unit) TIC = TER + TC EAC = EAC(IMC) + EAC(Advice) + EAC(Admin) + EAC(Other)

✅ Exam Tips & Key Rules

Calculator & Calculation Rules

  1. Always use BEGIN mode for income annuities (payments received at start of period)
  2. Never use resultant rate in the accumulation step (Step 3 always uses full investment return)
  3. Split income phases whenever different return rates apply to different time periods
  4. Education costs escalate faster than general inflation (typically 8-9% vs 5-7%)
  5. Use 365 days for all SA money market calculations
  6. NCD rates are NACQ; Discount instrument rates are NACA
  7. Convert EPS/DPS from cents to rands by dividing by 100

Report Writing Rules

  1. Vehicle choice and strategy are SEPARATE decisions
  2. Trust constraints matter: income-only trusts CANNOT make capital withdrawals
  3. Always state tax consequences for each recommendation
  4. Consider risk capacity AND time horizon when selecting return assumptions
  5. A financial plan is a living document — emphasise ongoing review
  6. Maximum 10 pages; Arial 12pt, 1.5 line spacing; client-specific, not generic
